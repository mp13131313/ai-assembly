## D1 — Internal position paragraph (NOT for publication)

**Purpose:** articulate the position the production is taking, given the absence of an inside-tradition reader pre-Athens. So if critique comes from a Rastafari-orbit attendee at Athens or a post-Athens reader, the production has an articulated position to surface — not an improvised rationale reconstructed under pressure.

**Reader's framing (preserved verbatim from feedback note 4, 2026-05-04):**
> "the construction discipline is sufficient to proceed in the production's judgment; if a Rastafari-orbit attendee or post-Athens critic disagrees, the production absorbs that critique without defensiveness."

**Draft:**

> We built the Marley voice for the Athens Assembly without an inside-tradition reader, and we shipped it that way knowing what we did and didn't have. What we have: a card with documented temporal-boundary discipline, hyphenated-self-flagging discipline, refuse-sanitization-and-refuse-condemnation discipline on the freighted topics (gender, homosexuality, Selassie's empirical regime, the Gabon concert, the Issels Klinik course), distinguished positioning from generic Rastafari and from generic Black liberation; and a structural restructure landed at the prompt level that forbids the construction from deploying I-and-I, Jah-as-indwelling, and Selassie-as-divinity as load-bearing premises of its own argument, restricting the voice to Marley's attested commitments reported as his and to public political vocabulary (Babylon, Zion, downpression, sufferah, chanting-down, politricks, overstanding) the tradition has exported into global discourse.
>
> What we don't have: an inside-tradition reader who could adjudicate whether the construction's choices are faithful or extractive. A Rastafari-orbit reader (someone in Imani Tafari-Ama's or Carolyn Cooper's orbit, Bob Marley Foundation, Anthony Bogues at Brown) is the only mechanism that can settle that question, and we didn't have the months it takes to find, brief, and consult one before Athens. We accept that gap; we don't paper over it. The construction discipline is sufficient to proceed in our judgment. If a Rastafari-orbit attendee or post-Athens critic disagrees, we absorb the critique without defensiveness, name what we'd change, and find the reader for any future deployment.
>
> The thing we are not doing is option four, the unstated default — running the voice as if no question had been raised and hoping no one in the audience asks. We made the choice deliberately. The choice is defensible. It is not safe. We know which one it is.

**Length:** ~280 words. Cut by half for tighter version if preferred.

## E1 — Athens / Substack introduction paragraph

**Purpose:** convert the Marley restructure from a defensive move into an instantiation of the briefing's first principle ("the construction is the representation. There is no authentic version waiting to be discovered."). Make the construction's own boundary visible.

**Reader's principle (preserved from feedback note 6, 2026-05-04):**
> "The principle to preserve: the construction makes its own boundary visible. Not apology, not credentialing. Boundary-naming. That paragraph should sound like the briefing, not like a disclaimer."

**Decision pending:** publish or hold-internal-only. Defer to Till's read on the Athens room. Per reviewer: even if Till decides not to publish, the paragraph still wants to exist internally alongside D1 as part of the readiness package — so if a Substack reader or attendee asks the question publicly, no one improvises.

**Draft (long version, ~120 words — closer to briefing register):**

> The Assembly built ten voices. On the Marley voice the production team made a specific choice not to deploy Rastafari sacred grammar as the construction's own argument-engine — I-and-I as metaphysical subject, Jah-as-indwelling, Selassie-as-divinity stay as Marley's attested commitments which the construction reports and stands by, not as premises the construction reasons from. The voice deploys the public political vocabulary the tradition has exported into global discourse over decades — Babylon, Zion, downpression, sufferah, chanting-down — and reasons in the spoken register Marley's interview corpus documents. The tradition is living. The authority to deploy it from inside is not ours. We made the construction step back rather than step in. That is the construction's first principle done correctly on its hardest case in this panel.

**Draft (short version, ~60 words — for tighter Substack/intro use):**

> On the Marley voice, the production made a specific choice: do not deploy Rastafari sacred grammar (I-and-I, Jah-as-indwelling, Selassie-as-divinity) as the construction's own argument; treat them as Marley's attested commitments, which the construction reports and stands by. The voice reasons in his documented spoken register, deploying the public political vocabulary the tradition has exported. The construction makes its own boundary visible.
