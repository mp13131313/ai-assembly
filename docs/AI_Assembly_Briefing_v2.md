## Why the project exists

The AI Democracy Marathon asks whether democracy can be extended to the more-than-human world. The question is live, morally real, and structurally difficult. It implicates ecological survival, indigenous rights, AI governance, intergenerational justice, and the basic architecture of representation. It is being addressed seriously across the Marathon's four days through walks, assemblies, readings, panels, and rituals — all human-led.

The Assembly exists to do one additional thing: to put the question under a specific kind of pressure by introducing non-human participants into the conversation overnight, and seeing what happens. Not as metaphor. As participants whose output joins the human conversation the following day and potentially changes it. A river and an octopus and Plato and Hannah Arendt and eight other voices read what the humans talked about during the day, produce their own artifacts overnight, and arrive at breakfast as things the humans can read, argue with, be unsettled by, or dismiss.

The question the Assembly tests is not "can an AI really channel a river?" It tests something narrower and more honest: what happens to a conversation about more-than-human democracy when something non-human actually enters the deliberation. Whether the conversation becomes *more-than*. And — equally important — what it means if it doesn't. If expansion happens, the theoretical argument for more-than-human democracy has an empirical leg under it. If it doesn't, that theoretical argument has to reckon with the difficulty of its own demonstration, even with the most sympathetic possible audience.

This is the structure the Aarhus provotype tradition formalised in 1992 and the DIS community developed in 2012: an artifact that exists to provoke informed critique of a possible future condition. The Assembly is a provotype of structural possibility. Its success and its failure are both findings.

There is a further argument the provotype makes simply by existing. Every future more-than-human democracy will face the same condition as this one: the representative of the river will be a construction. The agent for future generations will be constructed. The voice for the more-than-human in any room, ever, will be made by someone, with choices, using infrastructure. There is no authentic version waiting to be discovered. The construction is the representation. The Assembly does not simulate this condition. It instantiates it.

## The core question

***What happens to a conversation about more-than-human democracy when something non-human actually enters the deliberation and shares their opinion? Does it become more-than? And what does it mean if it doesn't?***

Not: did the Assembly produce interesting artifacts. Not: did people talk about the Assembly. Those are prerequisites. The real question is whether the conversation about everything else — about democracy, about AI, about organisational life, about Athens, about the polycrisis — reaches places by Day 4 it would not have reached if the Assembly had never existed. Whether it becomes more than what 750 humans alone, however thoughtful, could have produced. And, critically, whether the absence of expansion is itself a finding the project is built to surface honestly.

This is the three-layer test from the original briefing, restated:

**Layer 1 — Encounter.** The Assembly's overnight output has to be compelling enough that attendees actually engage with it. If attendees read the first Substack read-through and never click the second, the provotype has failed its first bar.

**Layer 2 — Content.** The Assembly's artifacts have to say something the humans in the room couldn't have said. Not just provocative — specifically, something where the attribution to a non-human voice matters. A river thinking about time the way a river thinks about time, not a human rhetorical performance about rivers. The octopus responding through embodied perception rather than philosophical argument. If the content could have come from a well-read human essayist, the provotype has reduced to an art project and lost its experimental leg.

**Layer 3 — Expansion.** Does the conversation become *more-than*. This is the hardest bar and the most consequential one. It is the only one that would matter to Arendt or Chwalisz or Akomolafe. It is also the one the audience is least capable of honestly assessing in real time, because this audience is professionally trained to absorb new material into existing frames without being changed by it. The closing show exists in part to make this test visible — to show the audience, in one moment, what the Assembly's positions across three nights actually amounted to, and to ask whether any of it actually carried the conversation somewhere it could not have gone alone. Equally: if it didn't, that absence is itself a finding the project is built to surface honestly, not paper over.

## The panel

Twelve voices, cast across the two axes of the casting matrix (mode of knowing: conceptual ↔ embodied; scope of perspective: individual ↔ beyond-human) and additionally balanced against the diversity checklist (civilisational tradition, gender, race, economic position, colonial position, relationship to the demos).

**The twelve:**

| Voice | Mode of knowing | Scope of perspective |
|---|---|---|
| Plato | Conceptual | Individual → beyond-human (via the Forms) |
| Cleopatra | Conceptual | Individual (statecraft) |
| Ibn Battuta | Conceptual + embodied | Individual (travel) |
| Scheherazade | Embodied (narrative) | Individual → collective (via story) |
| Ada Lovelace | Conceptual | Individual (machine cognition) |
| Dostoevsky | Conceptual + embodied | Individual (moral psychology) |
| Hannah Arendt | Conceptual | Collective (the public) |
| Bob Marley | Embodied (musical) | Collective (diaspora, liberation) |
| Audrey Tang | Conceptual | Collective (deliberative practice) |
| Peter Thiel | Conceptual | Individual (sovereign) |
| Whanganui River | Embodied | Beyond-human (ecological) |
| Octopus | Embodied | Beyond-human (sensory/other intelligence) |

The panel distributes across all four quadrants of the casting matrix. Conceptual voices are balanced by embodied ones. Individual-perspective voices are balanced by collective and beyond-human voices. The diversity checklist has been held against the composition — several dimensions are consciously held rather than fully covered (the panel has known gaps around contemporary indigenous cosmologies and around voices from outside the Western / West-Asian / East-Mediterranean axis). Those gaps are documented design decisions, not oversights.

The panel includes deliberate friction. Thiel and Arendt in the same room. Plato and the Octopus on the same provocations. Marley's musical mode alongside Lovelace's conceptual-mathematical one. The friction is itself part of the casting logic — a panel where every voice aligns produces predictable output.

## The casting matrix

The casting matrix is the internal tool that structured the panel. It maps each voice along two axes:

**Horizontal — mode of knowing.** Conceptual at one end; embodied at the other. How does this voice think? Through analysis and systems, or through practice and sensation?

**Vertical — scope of perspective.** Individual at one end; beyond-human at the other. What perspective does this voice *channel*? A human philosopher reasoning about the individual soul sits at the individual end. A human narrator whose work dissolves the boundary between self and world moves toward the beyond-human end. A non-human organism that experiences the ocean as its body sits at the beyond-human end for a different reason entirely.

The selection criterion throughout has been: *what perspective would be missing without you?* Not: *are you a valid political subject?* The casting matrix is a diversity-of-knowing instrument. It structurally prevents the panel from collapsing into one cognitive mode.

The original briefing's full argument for the matrix — including the diversity checklist and the reasoning for the individual/beyond-human axis framing — stands and does not need to be repeated here. This section exists to confirm that the matrix is the internal tool that was used, and that the panel was composed against it.

The casting matrix is not audience-facing. It does not appear at the closing show. It does not appear on the Substack or the micro-site. It is a design artifact that the project used and that can be cited in any subsequent discussion of how the panel was composed, but it is not part of what the audience encounters.

## The two matrices for the closing show

A separate pair of matrices is used at the closing show to map where the Assembly's positions landed. Both matrices are named in the Provocateur specification and used by the Provocateur during Selection to check that surviving themes cover different parts of the political-conceptual territory. At the closing show, they do the inverse: they receive the Assembly's actual positions and show where the Assembly landed across that territory.

**Matrix A — Power & Agency.**
- Horizontal: human ↔ nonhuman
- Vertical: many ↔ one

This matrix asks: *who holds authority, and what kind of entity wields it?* The four quadrants: human-many (classical democracy), human-one (autocracy), nonhuman-many (distributed nonhuman intelligence or ecological governance), nonhuman-one (sovereign AI or single ecological representative).

**Matrix B — Change & Actor.**
- Horizontal: rupture ↔ progression
- Vertical: individual ↔ collective

This matrix asks: *what kind of change, and through what unit of action?* The four quadrants: rupture-individual (heroic individual break), rupture-collective (revolution), progression-individual (personal practice, everyday ethics), progression-collective (slow institutional reform).

A single position can be plotted on both matrices. The pair locates a position more fully than either alone: *rule by the many, through nonhuman means, achieved through collective rupture* is a specific political stance that neither matrix alone would capture.

The matrices are not populated with one mass of 36+ position-points each. They are populated **per theme** — see the closing-show section below for how the theme-first approach works.

## The overnight pipeline

The Assembly's overnight process runs in five stages: audio capture to clean transcripts, Researcher, Provocateur, Voice Pipeline (three steps), and Downstream (render, publish, curate, deliver). The original briefing's Interpreter and Synthesiser stages are not in the nightly pipeline — they have been compressed into the closing show on Day 3 evening and appear there as a single mapped read-through.

The full orchestration, error handling, and API routing is specified in `AI_Assembly_Architecture_v1.md`. This section describes what each stage does and why, without repeating the technical specification.

### Stage 0 — Normalisation

The Marathon's daytime sessions produce recordings across multiple venues, speakers, and formats. Before the Researcher can work, these have to become clean, speaker-attributed transcripts. The transcription pipeline uses AssemblyAI Universal-3 Pro for ASR and speaker diarisation, with no upstream audio enhancement (modern ASR degrades on enhanced audio). Speaker identification runs through multi-pass LLM attribution against pre-loaded rosters and session metadata. A final LLM pass cleans the transcript while preserving substance and turn boundaries.

Full specification: `AI_Assembly_Transcription_Pipeline.md`. Orchestrated by Prefect (not n8n, because n8n cannot handle the audio-stage file sizes and concurrency). Output handed off to the rest of the pipeline as files in a shared folder.

### Stage 1 — Researcher

The Researcher is the first AI agent in the overnight pipeline. It is unbiased — it does not know the council's composition, the audience's profile, or the closing-show matrices. It captures faithfully what the day's sessions contained.

It works in two steps. First, extraction: it reads each transcript and pulls atomic positions, reframings, and open questions into a structured table. Second, grouping: it clusters all extractions across all sessions into themes using a modified KJ Method (affinity diagramming). Each theme has a title, a one-sentence abstract, and underlying cluster structure.

The Researcher runs fresh each night. On Night 2 and Night 3, it does not receive previous nights' output. Full specification: `AI_Assembly_Researcher_Pipeline.md`.

### Stage 1b — Provocateur

The Provocateur is the second AI agent. Unlike the Researcher, it is strategic and editorial. It knows the council — as a collective landscape and as individual profiles — and it knows the audience. It decides what gets asked, how, and who answers.

It works in four steps. Triage scans every theme the Researcher produced and tags each with which council members would activate on it and how strongly, whether it hits audience friction, and whether it opens a council fault line. Selection filters the triaged set, balancing for territory spread (using Matrix A and Matrix B to check coverage), per-member coverage (~3 themes per voice with at least one in the voice's stretch), and dropping themes that would produce weak responses. Formulation writes a question (default) or proposition (exception) per selected theme, aimed at the voices assigned to it. Packaging assembles per-voice briefing packages.

Critical fact about formulations: **one formulation per selected theme, sent to all voices assigned to that theme.** Plato and Arendt both assigned to a theme on cognitive sovereignty receive the same formulation. They reason independently in Step 1, but they are answering the same question.

A formulation may go to 2 voices or 8 depending on how broadly activating the theme is. A voice receives ~3 formulations (one per theme it was tagged on). The Assembly is therefore structured as a lattice of overlapping theme-groups: each theme is engaged by 2–8 voices, each voice engages ~3 themes, and the overlaps create the deliberation structure for Step 3.

On Night 2, the Provocateur receives Night 1's formulations and assignments so it does not repeat territory per member. On Night 3, it receives both previous nights. Full specification: `AI_Assembly_Provocateur_Pipeline.md`.

### Stage 2 — Voice Pipeline (three steps)

Each council member runs a three-step voice pipeline. The original briefing described a one-step model (voice receives formulation, produces artifact). The three-step architecture is a deliberate improvement.

**Step 1 — Private Reasoning.** The voice receives its per-voice briefing package with ~3 formulations. For each formulation, the voice produces a detailed response — reasoning through the question, landing on a committed position, voicing that position in the voice's natural mode but in a reasoning register. At ~3 formulations per voice, each voice produces ~3 detailed responses per night. Across the panel, that is ~36 detailed responses per night.

The system prompt for Step 1 is built from the voice's Persona Card, using the Foundational + Reasoning + Engagement field groupings. The voice reasons as itself, with its constitution and knowledge boundaries and characteristic methods, and lands somewhere.

**Step 2 — First-Draft Artifact.** The voice reads back its own detailed responses and produces one artifact that compresses across them. The artifact is the voice's public expression — the thing the audience eventually reads. It is not a direct answer to any single formulation. It is the voice's compressed expression of what it arrived at across the full set of formulations it engaged.

This step does two things. First, it improves artifact quality: the voice reasons fully before expressing, rather than doing reasoning and expression simultaneously. Second, it preserves voice autonomy in focus-selection: the voice decides what matters most to express publicly. A voice that engaged three formulations may lean into one, integrate two, or synthesise all three, based on what produces its strongest artifact.

The Step 2 system prompt uses the Foundational + Voice + Artifact field groupings from the Persona Card. Medium is decided at the voice level — dialogue for Plato, song for Marley, shader parameters for the Octopus, and so on.

**Step 3 — Amended Artifact.** Once all 12 voices have produced first-draft Step 2 artifacts, each voice reads the first-draft artifacts of the other voices it shares at least one theme with. The reading is framed by the shared themes — for each shared-theme artifact, the voice is pointed at the portion of the other voice's artifact that engages that theme.

The voice then decides whether to amend its own artifact. It may sharpen a disagreement ("Plato said X on this question; I disagree because Y, so my artifact needs to engage that position directly"). It may integrate a stronger framing that another voice opened up. It may distance its own position from a misreading. It may leave its artifact unchanged if it holds up in light of what the others produced.

If the voice amends, the amendment is visible — it references the other voice, even implicitly, so the amendment reads as responsive. If the voice does not amend, the Step 2 artifact publishes unchanged. A metadata flag records which voices amended and in response to which other voices' positions.

This is where the Assembly's collective character is constituted overnight. It is constrained — voices respond at the artifact layer, not the reasoning layer, and only within shared-theme territory — but it is real deliberation. The voices are in dialogue with each other on the questions they were given, and the published set is responsive to itself.

The voice pipeline's Steps 1 and 2 are specified in `AI_Assembly_Voice_Pipeline.md`. Step 3 is new architecture and requires specification before Athens.

### Stage 3 — Downstream (render, publish, curate, deliver)

After all voices complete Step 3, the amended artifacts move to the downstream pipeline. This runs in four sub-stages:

**Render.** Text artifacts pass through unchanged. Marley's artifact is a song specification sent to the Suno API, which returns an audio file. The Octopus's artifact is a JSON parameter block for a chromatophore shader that runs client-side on the micro-site. Multimedia voices are future-extensible as additional render branches.

**Publish.** All 12 rendered artifacts are committed as `night-N.json` to the micro-site's content repository. The static site rebuilds on commit, and each artifact is available at a permanent URL (`/night-1/plato`, `/night-1/marley`, `/night-2/arendt`, etc.).

**Curate.** A single Claude Opus call generates the night's Substack read-through — a walkthrough of each voice with a pull-quote and a deep link back to the micro-site, written in HoBB's editorial voice using real HoBB newsletters as few-shot exemplars. The read-through is authored by HoBB *about* the Assembly; the Assembly is reported on, not given direct editorial control. Nights 1 and 2 produce read-throughs; Night 3 skips this stage because the closing show reveals Night 2's output live and Night 3's final read-through belongs to the Day 4 morning goodbye.

**Deliver.** The Substack publishes. A blurb referencing the read-through lands inside HoBB's regular daily newsletter. The audience does not receive a separate Assembly newsletter.

### Stage 4 — Continuity

After Night 1 and Night 2 complete, a final pipeline pass generates per-voice continuity blocks that are inserted into the Persona Cards for the following night. Each continuity block records four fields: positions taken, key moves, unresolved threads, and artifact focus and stance. These feed the next night's Step 1 system prompts so that voices know what they themselves committed to on previous nights — individual continuity, not cross-voice continuity.

## Delivery architecture

The Assembly appears to the audience through five surfaces:

**The daily HoBB newsletter.** The existing once-a-day newsletter HoBB sends. A short blurb inside it, on Day 2, Day 3, and Day 4 mornings, points the audience to that morning's Substack read-through. Not a separate Assembly newsletter — one section within HoBB's normal daily rhythm.

**The Substack read-through.** On Day 2 and Day 3 mornings, a HoBB-voiced walkthrough of the previous night's artifacts, published on the HoBB Substack. Pull-quotes, editorial framing, deep links to each voice's artifact on the micro-site. On Day 4 morning, the Substack carries the Night 3 read-through alongside the Assembly's goodbye.

**The micro-site.** The permanent home of all 36 artifacts at stable URLs. Pre-built as an Astro or Next.js static site, content-driven from a GitHub repo, deployed to Vercel or Netlify with automatic rebuilds on commit. Organised by voice then by night. The Octopus's shader runs here. Marley's Suno audio plays here. The site stays live after the Forum ends.

**The closing show (Day 3 evening).** Two Assembly moments inside Act Five (*Beastopia*): a mapped read-through of 3–5 throughline themes populated across Matrix A and Matrix B, followed by video snippets of the Assembly's members running the last kilometre of the AI Democracy Marathon alongside the human hosts. Analytical first, embodied second. See the closing-show section below.

**The Day 4 goodbye.** A section inside the Day 4 daily newsletter, co-signed between HoBB editorial voice and one Assembly voice's final line. Lands on phones as attendees are leaving Athens — at the Community Picnic, in taxis to the airport, on the Aegean Passage boat. The Assembly's last word, read in motion.

Readers are self-selected: only those who click through from the daily newsletter read the Substack; only Substack readers visit the micro-site; only Day 3 evening attendees see the closing show in person. The closing show can be published to the micro-site afterwards for the rest. The four-day architecture is a funnel: each layer narrows the audience but deepens the encounter.

## The closing show

The AI Assembly appears in the closing show (Day 3 evening, Act Five, *Beastopia*) in two moments. Sequencing: mapped read-through first, video snippets second. The analytical substance lands first; the embodied payoff closes.

### The mapped read-through

On Day 3 afternoon, after Night 2's pipeline completes and before the closing show begins, two new pipeline passes run in sequence.

**Theme identification pass.** Reads across all three nights' Researcher themes, Provocateur formulations, and voice detailed responses. Identifies 3–5 throughline themes — the territories where the Assembly sustained substantive attention across nights, multiple voices engaged, and positions actually got taken. Thematic recurrence, formulation traction, and voice engagement aligned.

The throughlines are not predicted in advance. They emerge from what the Assembly actually did. Four of them might be something like *the legitimacy of nonhuman representation*, *the substrate of authority*, *rupture versus progression as response to the polycrisis*, and *the human-AI seam*. But the specific articulation waits for the output.

**Per-theme mapping pass.** For each throughline theme, reads each voice's detailed responses and artifacts to extract the voice's position on that theme. Plots each voice's position on Matrix A (Power & Agency) and Matrix B (Change & Actor). Identifies clusters (where did the Assembly concentrate?), divergences (who stood apart?), and empty quadrants (what did nobody occupy?). Generates a read-through script segment per theme.

### What the reveal looks like

The closing show's mapped read-through is a sequence of theme segments. Each segment:
- Names the theme
- Shows Matrix A populated with the Assembly's positions on this theme
- Shows Matrix B populated the same way
- Walks the clusters and divergences, names surprises
- Notes any empty quadrants the Assembly avoided

Three to five theme segments in a row. Consistent visual language across themes so the audience recognises the axes; different content per theme. Total length roughly 8–12 minutes depending on theme count and script pacing.

The read-through is narrated — either by one of the closing-show hosts (Till, Tim, or Meryem) or via prerecorded voiceover. The script is pipeline-generated first-draft, human editorial polish on Day 3 afternoon, final by early evening.

### What the reveal does

The mapped read-through does the structural-analytical work the closing show needs — and that the original briefing's Interpreter and Synthesiser stages were going to do across three mornings. Compressed into one moment, theme-first rather than night-first, it shows the Assembly's full position space at once.

It also does specific defensive work. Chwalisz's and Alexander's frameworks will read the Assembly through deliberation-theory terms: did positions get taken, did disagreements surface, did the collective produce something more than its parts. The mapped read-through is the place where those questions get visible answers. The answer is not that the Assembly produced consensus. The answer is that the Assembly produced a *mapped disagreement space* with visible clusters and visible empty quadrants, which is closer to what deliberation-theory actually recognises as collective output than a list of twelve monologues would be.

### The video snippets

After the mapped read-through, video snippets of the Assembly's members preparing to and running the last kilometre of the AI Democracy Marathon alongside Monique van Dusseldorp, Dave and Helen Edwards, and the other Marathon hosts. The humans finish the real Marathon on stage, live. On screen, the Assembly's members — in sweatshirts and running shoes — do the same, warming up, walking to the start line, running. Split-screen or intercut between live humans and screen Assembly.

Snippets rather than one continuous video: multiple short fragments, each self-contained, each under 45 seconds, distributed across this segment of the show. Easier to produce well than a single continuous 3–5 minute ensemble scene. Each snippet features one or a few voices in warm-up banter or mid-running conversation, referencing each other's artifacts and the humans running alongside them.

### How the video dialogue gets produced

A dedicated Video Pipeline pass runs on Day 3 afternoon alongside the theme identification and mapping passes. The pipeline:

**Input:** All 36 published artifacts (12 voices × 3 nights). Each voice's Persona Card. A Video Context block describing the staging — running shoes, sweatshirts, finish line, the humans running alongside, the fact that the voice is in a body it has never had.

**Process:** Per-voice generation of short spoken-dialogue fragments, each 15–45 seconds, in the voice's natural mode but in a spoken register. Each fragment is asked to reference at least one other Assembly voice's artifact or one of the running humans or the strangeness of the run itself.

**Output:** ~12 per-voice dialogue fragments, plus metadata indicating what each references.

**Editorial polish:** A 1–2 hour human pass (Till, Meryem, or a designated writer) cuts for deliverability. Generated text written for reading is often stilted when spoken aloud; the editorial layer catches that. Final fragments go to the closing-show production team for recording or animation.

This keeps the pipeline's voice-fidelity benefit while catching the spoken-versus-written translation problems pure generative output won't catch on its own. The voices are still producing their own material; the editorial hand is light and in service of performance, not substance.

### What the video does

The mapped read-through does the analytical argument. The video does the embodied one. A river in running shoes, the Octopus warming up, Plato jogging alongside Monique — this is the more-than-human demos made visible in the most literal way the Forum allows. Not as concept. As running image.

The sweatshirts are defensive. Cognitive-warfare critiques do not reach a river in sweatpants. The irreverence puts the Assembly outside the register in which the sharpest critiques operate. Seriousness without solemnity, HoBB-native.

The video also supplies the *Assembly* claim in its most concrete form. Twelve monologues delivered via Substack are not visibly an assembly, however much Step 3 amendment happens behind the scenes. Twelve members in shared frames, referencing each other, running alongside the humans — this looks like an assembly, sounds like one, closes the rhetorical gap.

### Visual deliverables

The Assembly project builds the closing-show visualisations end-to-end and hands them over to HoBB production as finished assets.

- Matrix-pair template designed once: axes, quadrant labels, typography, colour palette, the visual language.
- 3–5 populated instances, one per throughline theme, generated from the mapping pipeline output.
- Animated reveal sequence: theme named, matrices build in, positions plot, clusters visible, annotation.
- Exported as a video asset (probably a timed video file with audio cues for voiceover, or a silent video file the host narrates over live).
- Handover to HoBB production with file format, resolution, aspect ratio, and timing specs matched to the show's projection setup.

The video snippets are a separate deliverable — pipeline output, human editorial pass, then handoff to whichever production path HoBB chooses (live action with actors, animation, still-frame-plus-voiceover). Production decisions are the show team's; the dialogue content is ours.

## The four-day arc

The provotype's real test runs across accumulation. A single night's output can test the encounter and the content. Only the full four-day arc can test whether the conversation expands — or doesn't, in a way that itself constitutes a finding.

**Day 1.** The AI Democracy Marathon opens in the afternoon at the Pnyx and the Stock Exchange with the More-Than-Human Democracy assembly and the Mikro Pallas walk. The Forum itself opens that evening with Act One on the Main Stage. Somewhere in Day 1 — either inside the Marathon's opening session, at Act One, or split between both — the Assembly is introduced to the audience. The specific location of the introduction is a decision pending between Till and Matthias. See `Till_Briefing_The_AIssembly_Athens_2026_v2.md` for the four options and the reasoning.

**Day 1 night.** Night 1 runs. The audio pipeline cleans the day's transcripts. The Researcher extracts and groups themes. The Provocateur selects themes, writes formulations, packages per-voice briefings. Each voice runs Steps 1, 2, and 3. Artifacts are rendered, published to the micro-site, curated into a Substack read-through, delivered as a blurb in the Day 2 daily newsletter.

**Day 2 morning.** The daily newsletter lands. The Substack read-through goes live. Readers click through, read the Assembly's first round, visit the micro-site. Act Two on the main stage — Bruno Giussani's *The End of ~~Business~~ Humanity As We Know It* — begins at 8:45. If the introduction is at Act Two (one of the options), it happens here, as retrospective framing of what the audience has already encountered.

**Day 2 day + night.** Day 2's human sessions happen. The Researcher captures them including any references to the Night 1 artifacts. Night 2 runs identically to Night 1, with continuity blocks carrying forward each voice's Night 1 positions and the Provocateur avoiding territory per-member.

**Day 3 morning.** Day 3's daily newsletter and Substack read-through publish. The audience encounters the second round. The continuity in the artifacts is now visible — voices reference their own Night 1 positions, the Provocateur has moved to different formulations, the Step 3 amendments across the Assembly may have produced more inter-voice dialogue in the final published set.

**Day 3 afternoon.** Pipeline load: theme identification pass, per-theme mapping pass, video pipeline pass, human editorial polish for both the mapped read-through script and the video snippets. The visual assets are finalised. Handover to closing-show production by early evening.

**Day 3 evening — the closing show.** Act Five, *Beastopia*. The Assembly's two moments: mapped read-through first, video snippets second. This is the Assembly's peak dramaturgical moment. The analytical argument and the embodied argument both land in the same theatrical hour.

**Day 3 night.** Night 3 runs. Step 3 amendments happen against the most material the Assembly has accumulated — the voices have read the previous two nights, Night 3's formulations engage Day 3's sessions (including possibly reactions to the closing show itself), and the Step 3 amendments may reference both Night 3 artifacts and earlier nights.

**Day 4 morning.** The daily newsletter carries the goodbye blurb — HoBB editorial voice closing the relationship with the audience, quoting one Assembly voice's final line. The Substack publishes the Night 3 read-through. The full 36 artifacts are all now on the micro-site at their permanent URLs. The audience is mid-departure — at the Community Picnic, on the way to the airport, on the Aegean Passage boat. The Assembly's last word lands on phones in motion.

The arc is not a logistical feature of the conference schedule. It is the structure that separates a provotype from a one-night experiment. Every beat of it is doing specific work.

## What it is not

**Not a technology demonstration.** AI is the medium, not the message. It makes the encounter possible. It is not the point of the encounter. The Assembly would be a failure if the audience left talking about how impressive the AI was.

**Not a summary tool.** The Assembly does not organise or reflect back what humans already said. The default behaviour of any LLM system is to summarise; this Assembly is designed to do the opposite. If the artifacts merely reorganise Day 1's discussions into pretty prose, the provotype has failed.

**Not a consensus machine.** The Assembly's output is not agreement. It exposes tensions rather than resolving them. The interesting thing about a position is that you can disagree with it. The closing show's mapped read-through is designed to make disagreement visible, not to smooth it into a synthesised single voice.

**Not a claim to authentic non-human voice.** The Assembly is honest about what it is. When AI speaks as an octopus, it is not channelling an octopus. It is producing a designed provocation — a collision between source material and AI pattern-matching, shaped by whoever wrote the system prompt. The honesty is the point. The provotype's argument is that *all* future more-than-human representation will have the same constructed character; the Assembly instantiates that condition rather than hiding it.

**Not a tech layer bolted onto an embodied experience.** The Marathon's walks and rituals work because they are physical, slow, and ritualistic. The Assembly belongs to the same world. The overnight gap is not a technical constraint — it is the design. The closing-show video, with the members in running shoes, makes the belonging explicit.

**Not an alternative to human deliberation.** The Assembly does not replace the 750 humans' own work over four days. It runs alongside it. The question is whether adding non-human voices to the conversation changes what the humans can do together, not whether non-humans can do deliberation better or instead.

## Design principles

**Entertaining first, rigorous underneath.** The audience encounters presence and personality before they encounter conceptual architecture. Charisma earns attention; reasoning rewards it. This is why the medium of each voice matters — Marley produces a song, the Octopus produces a visual-somatic artifact, Plato produces a dialogue. The form is already content.

**Generative, not derivative.** Every output should pass the test: could the humans in the room have arrived here without this voice? If yes, the provotype has failed. This is the hardest design principle and the one that stresses every voice most directly at the persona-design level.

**Expose tensions, don't resolve them.** The Assembly does not seek consensus, does not tidy disagreements into clusters, does not present a unified view. The closing show maps disagreement as a feature, not a failure.

**The overnight gap is the design.** The exchange of arguments happens asynchronously: humans deliberate during the day, non-humans at night, the conversation is the back-and-forth across the gap. This shape is doing real work — it gives each side time to produce, it prevents real-time rhetorical gamesmanship, it makes the Assembly's output something the humans have to read and absorb rather than react to in the moment.

**An opinion demands a response.** Presence can be observed from a distance. An opinion demands that you agree, disagree, or reckon with why you're dismissing it. The Assembly puts humans in a position where ignoring the non-human voice becomes a conscious choice rather than a default.

**Belong to the texture of the Marathon.** The Assembly is strange in the way the funeral march is strange — designed strangeness that serves the inquiry. It occupies night the way the walks occupy day. The closing-show video, with the members running the last kilometre alongside the human hosts, makes the belonging literal.

**Make the construction visible.** Every layer of translation — from human discussion to Researcher extraction, from extraction to Provocateur formulation, from formulation to voice reasoning, from reasoning to artifact, from artifact to amended artifact, from amended artifact to published artifact, from published artifact to mapped position — is visible somewhere in the documentation, the editorial framing, or the closing show. Nothing is hidden. The audience is sophisticated enough to absorb the construction and judge the work anyway.

**Constitute the collective at specific moments, not continuously.** The Assembly's collective character is constituted at Step 3 (artifact-level inter-voice response, overnight, constrained to shared themes) and at the closing show (analytical mapping across the full set). It is not constituted at the reasoning layer (voices don't see each other's detailed responses) and it is not claimed beyond what the architecture actually produces.

## What success looks like

**Readers click through.** The daily newsletter blurb points to the Substack. If click-through rates on Day 2 and Day 3 are in the range HoBB expects for a genuinely engaging newsletter section, Layer 1 has passed. If nobody clicks, nothing downstream matters.

**The Assembly enters the conversation.** Attendees reference the Assembly's artifacts in the day's human sessions — naming a voice, quoting a position, arguing with something a voice said. The Researcher captures these references in Night 2 and Night 3's transcripts, which means Night 2 and Night 3 can respond to them. If the Assembly is being referenced, the encounter-to-content pipeline is working.

**The closing show lands.** The audience reads the mapped matrices as meaningful, the video snippets as funny-and-also-serious, and the combined moment as the closing show's actual closing argument rather than as a novelty. Post-show social media and the Day 4 Community Picnic conversations reference the Assembly's positions, not just the Assembly's existence.

**The Day 4 goodbye is read.** Attendees on planes and boats read the final Substack and the Assembly's last line. Some forward it. Some quote it in their own post-Forum writing. The Assembly's artifacts stay live on the micro-site and attract readers in the weeks after.

**Engagement is a finding.** If attendees accept the non-human voices as legitimate participants in the conversation — if they engage with the opinions, argue with them, let them change the direction of discussion — that is evidence about the conditions under which more-than-human governance becomes possible.

**Dismissal is a finding.** If even this audience — the most sympathetic imaginable — cannot bring themselves to take constructed non-human voices seriously, that is equally important evidence about the distance between theorising more-than-human democracy and actually living with it.

**Indifference is failure.** If the Assembly does not register — if people neither engage nor dismiss but simply don't read the Substack, don't quote the voices, don't remember the closing show — the provotype has failed its execution bar. Not the concept, but this instantiation of it. The encounter wasn't compelling enough to create the condition the experiment needs.

## The audience

The audience profile, reception analysis, positioning principles, and introduction-location decision are specified in detail in `Till_Briefing_The_AIssembly_Athens_2026_v2.md`. That document derives from this one for architectural specifics and adds audience-facing detail that is not needed here.

The short version: ~750 senior professionals across seven distinct intellectual factions, trusting HoBB's curation, wanting their contradictions named rather than resolved, whose professional skill is intellectual hospitality — which is also the provotype's deepest receptivity failure mode. They are capable of producing Layer 3 expansion and equally capable of *performing* expansion without being changed by it. The provotype is designed to surface which is happening.

The relevant architectural consequence for this document is that the Assembly has to be designed against the audience's hospitality instinct. The artifacts that succeed will be the ones the audience keeps returning to not because they were beautiful but because they were unresolved.

## What remains uncertain

A short list of things this architecture cannot pre-engineer:

- Whether the Plato artifact passes technical scrutiny by Quarch, Tsinorema, and Erinakis. Execution-layer question for the persona work, not positioning.
- Whether the Marley song (via Suno) lands in a musically literate room or becomes the proof point for every critic. Mark Aanderud as musical collaborator on the Suno output is the risk-mitigation path.
- Whether Audrey Tang attends and encounters her own simulated voice. If she is in the room reading her own artifact, what she says afterwards defines the reception.
- Whether the Thiel persona carries legal risk. Needs review separate from the architectural spec.
- Whether the closing-show video production can hit its Day 3 afternoon deadline. Live action vs animation has major production implications for the timeline.
- Whether the audience actually shifts over the four days, or performs shifting without being changed. The experiment's actual question — answerable only through the full run.
- Whether the detailed responses across 36 entries produce enough variance across Matrix A and Matrix B per theme to make the mapped read-through dramaturgically interesting. If every voice clusters in one quadrant on every theme, the reveal lands flat; if the positions spread with visible clusters, the reveal has stories to tell. Unknowable in advance.

These are uncertainties, not gaps. The experiment is built to surface them, not to pre-empt them.
