# The AI Assembly — Frame Concept v1

**World Beautiful Business Forum · Athens · May 7–10, 2026**

---

## What this document is

A reverse-engineered briefing for the framing layer of the AI Assembly. The Assembly's *substance* — twelve, now ten, voices producing artifacts overnight on questions raised in the Marathon — is specified across the existing pipeline documents and stabilised in the v3.1 briefing. This document specifies the *frame*: the fictional organisation through which those artifacts reach the audience, the surfaces it operates across, and what the frame is for.

The document is constructed in three layers. First, what the frame instantiates conceptually — reverse-engineered from the provotype's two conditions and the audience's deepest receptivity failure mode. Second, what the frame is, surface by surface — micro-site, newspaper, Substack, closing show, Day 4 goodbye. Third, what it implies operationally — pipelines, decisions made, decisions pending, risks. A reception analysis across the seven contributor factions sits between the second and third layers.

This is a v1 document. It supersedes nothing; the briefing v3.1 remains the source of truth for the Assembly itself. This document specifies *how the Assembly appears to the world*, which the briefing v3.1 had treated as an open architectural question.

This is a **parent document**. The frame applies across surfaces but each surface is its own production object with its own constraints, audience, and risks. The broadsheet, the micro-site, the closing show video, and the Day 4 goodbye each require their own mini-concept derived from this frame — short specifications that translate the general frame into the specific decisions that surface needs (layout, IA, beat sheet, voice calibration, fallback plans). This document does not attempt those translations; it specifies the frame they share.

## Why a frame at all

The Assembly produces artifacts attributed to entities that did not write them. Plato's dialogue is a dialogue Plato did not write. The Whanganui River's statement is a statement the river did not make. The Octopus's chromatophore shader is not the Octopus's. This is structurally true regardless of how the artifacts are produced or presented; it is the condition the project has chosen to inhabit.

A naturalistic presentation — avatars of the panel members, voiced reproductions, body doubles — would compound the problem rather than solve it. It would claim authentic non-human voice in the very act that disclaims it. It would also produce cringe: a CGI Plato, a synthetic Marley, a digitally rendered Cleopatra are aesthetic dead ends regardless of how well executed. They invite the audience to assess the impersonation, which is the wrong question. The right question is what the voice *says*.

A flat presentation — artifacts published with no frame, attributed to their voices, treated as if they were straightforwardly the voices' own — would do the opposite damage. It would hide the construction. It would ask the audience to suspend disbelief in a way the project has explicitly committed not to ask.

The frame solves both problems by being neither. It introduces a fictional institution — *the Assembly* as a reporting organisation in its own right — through which the artifacts reach the audience. The institution's form is news media of a parodic, mid-century, slightly menacing variety. The fictionality is announced from the first frame and never disclaimed. The artifacts inside the frame are real (real text, real positions, real disagreements). The frame around them is constructed (no such organisation exists; no such anchors exist; the broadsheet has never published before).

This is the same move that thought experiments make. The reader enters the fiction in order to do real cognitive work the fiction enables. The fiction is operational, not decorative.

## The provotype conditions the frame instantiates

The briefing v3.1 names two conditions the provotype tests. The frame does not introduce new conditions; it makes both visible.

**The first condition.** Every future more-than-human democracy will face the same problem: the representative of the river will be a construction. No authentic voice waits to be discovered. The frame instantiates this by making the construction the form of presentation. The audience does not encounter the river; they encounter *a paper that reports on what the river is said to have said*. The chain of mediation is the visible structure, not a fact buried beneath a naturalistic surface.

**The second condition.** Every future more-than-human assembly will be built by someone, with consequences for legitimacy and accessibility of representation infrastructure. The Assembly is the prosumer version. The frame instantiates this through aesthetic registers that match prosumer production conditions — pre-war broadsheet typography, mid-century newsroom grammar, anachronistic intrusions where useful. Not OpenAI's polished product. Not a national broadcaster. A community-television register for a community-television build. The frame and the production conditions agree.

Both conditions are tested by the same frame. The frame is not the experiment's setting; it is part of what the experiment proposes.

## The six conceptual moves the frame makes

These are not features of the design. They are structural arguments the frame is making, whether or not the audience consciously parses them.

**One: representation of the unavailable is the form.** Future generations cannot speak. Rivers cannot testify. Non-humans cannot give statements. Plato is dead. Their representation is always representation of an absence. The frame's running gag — *we tried to get them on tape; we could not; here are the remnants* — is the most accurate visual form for what these voices structurally are. Traces, not testimonies. The fact that the news crew is always late is the fact that the more-than-human is always already past the camera. The comedy is not decorative; it is the form of the problem.

**Two: the construction is the representation.** The frame's news-media form is the most constructed possible visual register. Anchors, chyrons, lower-thirds, datelines, masthead engraving, paratext that announces "Vol. CXIV. No. 39,288" — every element of the form is an explicit announcement that this is constructed reportage. Adopting it explicitly says: this is what constructed representation looks like, here is what it would look like at industrial scale. The frame's first-order claim and the briefing's first-order claim are the same claim.

**Three: the newsroom is the prosumer condition.** Two non-technical people, Claude, rented compute — the visual analogue is two people in a fake newsroom, with cardboard sets and B-roll of guitars. The form's slight cheapness is not concealed; it is the production condition speaking through the production design. Where the form is slightly off — where the typography is too heavy, where the anchor's lip-sync wavers, where the masthead's claim of long pedigree is obviously confected — the form is being honest about who built it and how.

**Four: the Assembly is itself a reporting organisation.** The Assembly reports on the Marathon, refracted through ten voices. The fictional Assembly News reports on the Assembly. The recursion is the point. Layer upon layer of "we are bringing you what was said elsewhere" makes visible that all democratic representation is a chain of reportage. The voice in the chamber speaks for the people who were not in the chamber. The Assembly speaks for the entities that are not in the chamber. The news anchor speaks for the Assembly. None of these voices is the entity it speaks for. The frame makes this chain readable as a single structure.

**Five: the fiction is operational, not decorative.** The audience is asked to enter a fiction in which the voices are real and reported on, while knowing the entire frame is constructed. This is exactly how thought experiments work. The Assembly is a thought experiment the audience inhabits for four days. The matrices at the closing show are the analytical output of having taken the thought experiment seriously for ninety-six hours.

**Six: the HoBB newsletter is reality-testing.** The Substack stays in HoBB voice. HoBB is real. The newsletter is real. When the newsletter writes earnestly about what the Assembly published last night, it treats the *substance* as real (which it is — ten voices took positions on real questions) while accepting that the *form* is fictional (which it is — there is no real Assembly News). The reader oscillates between fictional frame and real-substance bridge. The oscillation is where Layer 3 expansion can happen. The newsletter is what prevents the frame from collapsing into a pure gag.

A seventh move emerges from the broadsheet treatment specifically: **the form of reportage can itself be voiced.** Headline grammar for the Plato story is *Gorgias*-shaped; headline grammar for the Dostoevsky story is confession-shaped; headline grammar for the river is wire-service-terse. The frame is not neutral; the reportage is voiced too. This is a richer surface than a single house style, and it is what makes the broadsheet form structurally stronger than a uniform broadcast frame for the print and web layers.

## How the frame works against the three layers

The briefing v3.1 specifies a three-layer test: Encounter, Content, Expansion. Each layer asks something different, and each is harder than the one before. The frame's contribution to each is uneven, and naming the unevenness honestly is part of how this document avoids overclaiming.

**Layer 1 — Encounter.** This is where the frame does most of its work. The frame's aesthetic register — pre-war broadsheet typography, mid-century news pastiche, paparazzi gag, weatherman deadpan, the slight chill of *Vol. CXIV. No. 39,288* — is calibrated to earn the audience's first attention. The Substack read-through, the front page, the closing-show video are designed to be encountered. The frame's surfaces compete with everything else on the audience's phone and in the conference programme; the broadsheet form and the news-pastiche form are more legible as objects-worth-engaging-with than flat-published artifacts would be. The frame is not just decoration here; it is the encounter device. Without the frame, the same artifacts published flat would have a much harder time clearing Layer 1.

**Layer 2 — Content.** Content lives in the artifacts themselves; the frame's job at this layer is mostly *not to obscure*. The micro-site artifact pages must let the dialogue, the song, the shader, the essay arrive unmediated. The Substack must quote and engage with the artifacts directly, not the broadsheet's reportage of them. Where the frame *does* work at this layer, it does it through the headlines — the Plato story's headline references the *Gorgias*; the Dostoevsky sidebar uses confession-grammar; the wire-service "Whanganui River arrives; says nothing" reports a content-layer fact about how the river relates to the form of statement. Each headline is a 9-word substance test. The frame-breakdown at the river and Octopus in the closing show is also content-layer work — saying something about the more-than-human that humans could not have said in the same form. But the bulk of Layer 2 is artifact work, which the frame must protect rather than produce.

**Layer 3 — Expansion.** Whether the conversation about more-than-human democracy reaches places it would not have without the Assembly. The frame's contribution at this layer is mostly oblique. The frame gets the audience to Layer 1 so that Layer 3 *can* happen; it does not produce expansion directly. The mechanisms that could produce expansion live elsewhere: in the artifacts (Layer 2), in the Substack's substantive engagement (the bridge function), in the matrices' showing of real disagreement (the closing show), in accumulation across three days (the four-day arc). The frame supports each of these but produces none of them. The frame-puncture move — one anchor breaking frame to name the hospitality failure mode — is the only direct Layer 3 device the frame contains, and it is small relative to the layer's difficulty.

The honest summary: **the frame is most load-bearing at Layer 1, supporting at Layer 2, indirect at Layer 3.** This means the frame's success metric is whether it earns the audience's attention well enough that Layer 2 substance can do Layer 3 work. The frame is the door, not the room. If the frame is over-built, the door becomes the destination — which is the failure mode the audience's hospitality instinct would prefer. If the frame is under-built, the audience never opens the door. Calibration at every surface is between these two.

## The architecture: three surfaces, one organisation

The Assembly appears to the audience through three surfaces, plus the closing show and the Day 4 goodbye. The three surfaces are described here; the closing show and goodbye in subsequent sections.

The three surfaces resolve a tension that the project sat with through several iterations: the artifacts must be taken seriously and engaged with directly, *and* the frame must be visible and sustained, *and* a real-voice bridge must exist between fiction and audience life. None of these can be the wrapper of the others. Each gets its own surface.

### Surface 1 — The micro-site (the artifacts, plain)

The micro-site is where the artifacts live as themselves. Plato's dialogue is the dialogue. Marley's song plays. The Octopus's chromatophore shader runs in the browser. Arendt's essay reads as an essay. Each artifact at a permanent URL (`/night-1/plato`, `/night-2/arendt`). No newspaper chrome wraps the artifact. No anchor introduces it. The artifact arrives unmediated.

The micro-site does carry a small amount of frame — masthead identity (the Assembly, the ten-pointed star device) appears on every page; navigation that uses the fictional organisation's vocabulary ("Tonight's Edition," "Voice Index") sits on the index pages. But the artifact pages themselves are stripped: a small masthead identifier at the top, then the artifact. No dateline metadata interceding between the reader and the dialogue. No fictional-organisation vocabulary wrapping the substance. A reader who arrives at `/night-1/plato` from a forwarded link encounters the masthead identifier and then Plato's dialogue, with no further frame in between. The frame is the wallpaper of the index pages; on the artifact pages it is barely a watermark.

This is the structural commitment. The artifacts are inviolate. They are what the experiment produces. They are what Layer 3 expansion, if it happens, will happen because of. The frame must not stand between the audience and the substance.

### Surface 2 — The newspaper (the Assembly's self-reportage)

Once per night, a single front-page broadsheet is published as its own artifact, alongside the ten voice artifacts. This is the Assembly News — *The Assembly* as both the panel and its own publication. The paper does the work of the fictional organisation: headlines per voice, short reportage paragraphs, wire-service unavailability lines, occasional strikethroughs as signature visual move, masthead with incrementing issue number across the three editions.

The paper does not reproduce the artifacts. It reports on them. "Mr. Plato's full address — including his remarks on knack and craft — printed in full at thessembly.org/night-1/plato." This is exactly how a real newspaper handles long-form material from elsewhere. The paper points; the artifact is the destination.

The aesthetic register of the broadsheet is pre-war American/British newspaper — Caslon-style headlines, multi-deck headers, drop caps, two-column body, rule lines, dense paratext. Roughly 1935–1955 in visual reference, with the masthead claiming a much longer history than such a paper could plausibly have ("Vol. CXIV. No. 39,288"). The fictional pedigree is part of the joke and part of the gravitas. A paper that has published 39,288 issues is not a paper one easily dismisses, even when one knows it does not exist.

The paper is *one of eleven artifacts per night*, not the wrapper of the other ten. This is the structural decision. It means a reader can engage with all eleven, or with the ten and not the paper, or with the paper and a few of the ten. The paper does not gatekeep access to the artifacts.

The headlines on the front page do specific work. Each voice has a **headline poetics** — a small specification of what the headline grammar for that voice sounds like. Plato gets *Gorgias*-style rhetorical questions. Dostoevsky gets affectionate-toward-suffering compound headlines, occasionally with strikethrough. The river gets wire-service terseness ("WHANGANUI RIVER ARRIVES; SAYS NOTHING"). Marley gets song-title-grammar. The Octopus gets headlines that drift toward chromatic or sensory description. Each voice's headline is doing two jobs: reporting what they said, and demonstrating in nine words that the writer of the headline was, briefly, in the voice's register. The headline is the smallest possible Quarch-clearance test, and the broadsheet front page passes it ten times per edition.

Two calibrations bound the broadsheet's frame load. The wire-service unavailability paragraph (the Octopus could not be reached for comment, etc.) is the broadsheet's pure-frame element; the front page carries at most one such paragraph per edition, not one per voice. The strikethrough is the paper's signature visual move; it appears at most once per edition. Both elements get sparingly used because used densely they convert the front page from "voiced reportage with one quirk" into "the strikethrough paper" — the form takes over from the content. The headlines themselves do not count against this load because each headline is a 9-word substance test (Quarch-clearance, voice register, what the artifact actually said), which is the strongest possible position any frame element can occupy.

### Surface 3 — The HoBB Substack (the bridge)

The Substack stays in HoBB voice. This is the only surface that does not enter the fiction. HoBB is the real organisation; the Substack is its real publication; it engages with the Assembly's outputs the way HoBB would engage with any object worth engaging with.

This means HoBB reads the artifacts directly — not the broadsheet's reportage on them, the artifacts themselves — and writes about them substantively. "Last night the Assembly published its first edition. The dialogue from Plato runs about four pages and turns on a question from the *Gorgias*; what struck us reading it was…" The Substack engages with substance because the substance is what makes the Assembly worth taking seriously, and HoBB is the surface whose function is to communicate that the project takes itself seriously.

The Substack's pull-quotes come from the artifacts, not from the broadsheet's reportage of them. This is the calibration that holds the bridge function. If the Substack quoted the broadsheet — "the Assembly's front page reports that Plato said…" — the bridge would be at one remove from substance, and the frame would have crept in. HoBB reads what Plato actually said; HoBB writes about what Plato actually said; HoBB pull-quotes Plato. The broadsheet exists, HoBB knows it exists, HoBB can mention it; but HoBB does not route through it.

HoBB also references the broadsheet in passing — "the Assembly's own front page treats it differently; that's at thessembly.org/night-1/edition" — so curious readers know the fictional layer exists. But HoBB does not adopt the fictional voice. The bridge holds because the bridge is real.

The Substack on Day 2 morning and Day 3 morning does this work for Night 1 and Night 2 artifacts respectively. The Day 4 morning Substack belongs to the goodbye, which is described below.

The audience self-sorts across the three surfaces. The reader who wants the substance straight reads the Substack and clicks into artifacts. The reader who wants the world the Assembly has built reads the broadsheet and clicks into artifacts. The reader who arrives at the artifact directly from a search or a forwarded link reads the artifact alone. All three reach the same substance. The frame is available, not imposed.

## The closing show: the newsreel division

Day 3 evening, Act Five (*Beastopia*). The closing show is a five-minute video, all AI-generated, embedded in the closing segment of the AI Democracy Marathon track. Monique van Dusseldorp closes the Marathon, and as part of that close says — in some form to be drafted — *let us check on the Assembly*. The video begins. The handoff is a check-in, not a reveal. The audience does not sit up to watch a film; they get tuned into the channel that has apparently been broadcasting in parallel for three days.

The video is the **newsreel division** of the same fictional Assembly that publishes the broadsheet. Pathé / Movietone-era visual register, mid-century newsroom typography, broadcaster identity continuous with the paper. An audience that has been reading the paper for three days will recognise the newsreel as the same organisation; an audience that has not will read the newsreel on its own terms. The connection is implicit, not laboured.

### Structure

Five minutes is tight, and how the five minutes are split matters more than the moves they contain. The closing show's deliberative payoff lives in the matrices; the frame's job is to earn the audience's attention to them. An earlier draft of this structure spent roughly 70% of runtime on the frame (cold open, paparazzi establishment, four voice unavailability scenes, hotel scene) and roughly 30% on the matrices. The right ratio inverts that.

The structure that fits five minutes:

- **Cold open and frame establishment (~30 seconds).** Chyron and masthead identity. *Late Night Edition. The Assembly is in session.* A single paparazzi-style B-roll shot integrated into the cold open, not a separate establishing segment. The audience needs maybe ten seconds to read the frame; thirty seconds is generous.
- **Three voice scenes, not four (~120 seconds total).** Plato as the celebrity gag (~30s, hotel and limousine, robes catching in the door). The Whanganui River as the first frame-breakdown (~45s, the crew arrives, there is a river, the crew is silent for a long beat, leaves). The Octopus as the second frame-breakdown (~45s, an aquarium spokesperson explains in deadpan that the aquarium does not have a comment system for octopuses). One celebrity gag plus two frame-breakdowns. The ratio is the argument: the frame admits its own bad fit at the more-than-human voices for two-thirds of the runtime allocated to voice scenes.
- **The weatherman segment (~150 seconds).** The analytical anchor reads the matrices. Three to five themes, each named and shown on both Matrix A and Matrix B, walking the cluster, the divergence, the empty quadrant. The frame-puncture line lives inside this segment, mid-readout, addressing the audience watching. This is where the closing show's deliberative weight rests; this is where Chwalisz reads evidence of mapped disagreement worth taking seriously. Two and a half minutes is what the matrices need to breathe; less and the readout is gestural, not substantive.
- **Anchor signoff to live statement (~30 seconds).** *We could not get a statement. They have provided one in writing. It will be read on stage.* Cut.

Net ratio: roughly 30% frame, 70% substance. Live on stage: Matthias reads the statement. The video has spent five minutes establishing that the panel members cannot be reached. The statement-reading fills the absence the video has constructed. Matthias is the builder; the body of the person who constructed the Assembly steps onto the stage in his own person to read what the construction produced. The second-order condition of the provotype becomes literal.

### The three voices on screen

Ten voices cannot all appear in five minutes; the runtime budget allows three voice scenes plus the matrix segment plus the cold open and signoff. The other seven voices appear in the matrix segment as plotted points on Matrix A and Matrix B. The three are chosen so that the *ratio* between celebrity gag and frame-breakdown does Akomolafe-defence work in real time:

- **Plato.** The civilisational human. Iconic, dead long enough that estate or likeness issues are nil. The paparazzi gag works hardest on him. Hotel lobby, robes catching in the limousine door as the car pulls away. ~30 seconds.
- **The Whanganui River.** The first frame-breakdown. The crew arrives at a riverbank. There is a river. The frame breaks. No chase, no gag, no sandal. The crew is silent for a long beat. Then they leave. ~45 seconds. This is the move that does Akomolafe-protection visibly. The frame is honest about its own bad fit with the more-than-human.
- **The Octopus.** The second frame-breakdown, and structurally different from the river. The crew arrives at an aquarium. A spokesperson says, in deadpan, that the aquarium does not have a comment system for octopuses. The frame breaks differently — not into silence but into bureaucratic refusal. The Octopus is unreachable not because she is hiding but because the categories of *reachable* and *unreachable* do not apply. ~45 seconds.

One scene where the frame works as celebrity gag; two scenes where the frame breaks intentionally and visibly. The two frame-breakdowns occupy two-thirds of the voice-scene runtime, which is the asymmetry the argument turns on. Bob Marley, considered earlier as a fourth voice with the empty-guitar-room image, is moved to the matrices and the micro-site. The song is the artifact; the song lives where it can be listened to. A 30-second video clip of an empty hotel room would not represent the song meaningfully and would cost the matrix segment durations it needs.

### Why this configuration

The frame's weakest point is its bad fit with the more-than-human voices. A paparazzi chase after the Whanganui River is not a paparazzi chase; it is a category error wearing the costume of one. Treating the river the same way as Plato would domesticate the more-than-human into the categories of human celebrity culture, which is the precise gesture Akomolafe calls a technology of humiliation. The defence is that the frame *acknowledges its own bad fit* — visibly, on screen, in the same five minutes — by breaking at the more-than-human voices for the majority of voice-scene runtime. The frame works for what it works for and admits where it does not. The admission is at scale, not gestural, which is what makes the frame defensible to the Posthumanist Reframers; without the runtime ratio, the admission would be theoretically clean and practically thin.

### Aesthetic specificity

Cronkite-era American network news + BBC earnest mode + Tagesschau, with anachronistic intrusions where useful (livestreams, colour, phones). RT was the conceptual reference for the *energy* — the slightly-off, slightly-menacing newsroom that does not quite look like real news — but is not the visual referent. The visual lineage avoids RT specifically because Faction G (Security / Geopolitics Realists) will read RT-styling as politically loaded. The print form is pre-war broadsheet, which gives the fictional organisation a longer pedigree than the broadcast form alone could carry.

### Production constraints

The video is all AI-generated. Anchor consistency across eight to ten cuts is the highest production risk and needs a pre-production test as early as possible. The newsreel typography, lower-thirds, chyron, and masthead identity are graphic-design work that does not come from the AI generator and must be art-directed separately. Music and foley are separate production layers. Five minutes of show-quality cuts is a real production effort, even with AI-generated source material; the script must be locked early enough to allow for retake budget if anchor consistency fails on the first pass.

## The Day 4 goodbye

The frame does not follow the audience out of the room. The Day 4 morning Substack returns to direct contact: HoBB voice + one panel voice's final line. Read on phones in motion — Community Picnic, airport, Aegean Passage boat. The audience has been through the frame for three days; on Day 4 they are in transit and need something simpler.

The panel voice chosen for the goodbye is decided in light of Night 3's artifacts. Candidates: the river, Arendt, Plato, Marley, or the Octopus. The choice will be context-dependent on what Night 3 produced. The structural commitment is that the goodbye is *outside the frame*: it is the panel voice in HoBB's editorial setting, reaching the audience as they move.

This means the frame has a beginning (Night 1, Substack and broadsheet, both arriving in the audience's morning) and an end (the closing show, Day 3 evening, the live statement reading). After the close, the frame closes too. What carries forward to Day 4 is the artifact and the bridge, not the fictional institution.

## How the frame meets the audience

### The hospitality failure mode

The audience brief identifies the deepest failure as performing reception — being so good at hospitality that they do not know when they are not being changed. The risk this frame must answer: the news-channel pastiche is *very* easy to perform reception on. *How witty, how HoBB, how paradoxical, how seriousness-without-solemnity.* The audience could absorb the entire show as a beautiful HoBB-native artefact and walk out unchanged.

The defences are several, and all live downstream of the frame rather than in it:

The matrices must show real disagreement that the audience has not predicted. The Day 3 afternoon mapping pass must find, per theme, a *surprise* — a voice that landed somewhere unexpected, an empty quadrant that should not be empty, two voices that converged where divergence was predicted. Without surprise, the matrices confirm that the panel was diverse, which is uninteresting and absorbable.

The live statement must be unresolvable. Whatever Matthias reads at the end of the closing show must be a position the audience can disagree with as they leave, not a moral. The frame ends in disagreement, not synthesis.

The artifacts on the micro-site must do the patient work of accumulation across three days. The audience that returns to read them on Day 2, Day 3, Day 4 is doing the substantive engagement the frame can only invite, not produce.

The Substack must engage substantively, not stylistically. HoBB writing in HoBB voice about what the artifacts actually said — not about how charming the broadsheet is — is what tells the audience that HoBB takes the substance seriously, which is the permission they need to take it seriously themselves.

One additional move the frame commits to: one of the news anchors, mid-matrix-readout, breaks frame for a single beat to address the audience directly. The move names the hospitality failure mode inside the show itself, in the show's own deadpan register. It is the closest thing to puncturing the audience's reception performance the show can do without becoming a lecture.

The exact line is drafted later and refined on tape. Three constraints for the drafting: one sentence under fifteen words; addresses *the audience watching* in the third person rather than *you* directly, because the slight grammatical chill of being named as a category is what makes the puncture work; returns to deadpan matrix-readout in the next line with no pause, no acknowledgement, no eye-flicker. Cronkite-deadpan, not TED-line. The audience is left holding the moment alone.

### Activation conditions

The frame activates this audience on several things the audience brief lists explicitly: paradox as rhetorical structure, philosophical and poetic registers as primary, spiritual without devotional, aesthetic decisions as content. It also activates on *seriousness without solemnity*, which the audience brief names as HoBB's distinguishing capacity and the failure mode for outsiders. The frame is structurally that capacity in operation.

The frame goes flat if the substance behind the headlines is thin, or if the matrices show predictable diversity rather than real surprise, or if the closing-show video reads as polished spectacle rather than as a constructed argument.

### Faction-by-faction read

**Cognitive Sovereignty Hawks** (Véliz, Giussani, Kasket, Jackson, Edwards). Sharpest read: by making the construction visible through humour, the frame absolves itself of the very critique it claims to be naming. *You showed me an AI panel and made jokes about it; the autonomous-thought question hasn't moved.* The defence is the matrices and the substance, not the frame. If the matrices show real disagreement between voices on questions of cognitive sovereignty itself, the substance does the work the frame cannot. Véliz's *Prophecy* publishes sixteen days before the Forum opens. Giussani is hosting Act Two. Kasket has already publicly dismantled Hotwire on HoBB territory. Armed and present.

**Sovereign AI Builders** (White, Bhattacharya, Giacomelli, Sethumadhavan, Lappas, Glassenberg). Likely to read the frame as *underselling* — implying the project did not dare to do AI-mediated representation earnestly. The mitigation is that the frame is honestly self-positioning as prosumer; it is a confident statement of what the build is, not a hedge. This faction is likely friendly to the substance and ambivalent about the frame.

**Posthumanist Reframers** (Akomolafe, Bridle, Johar, Hamzaçebi, Miller, Papadopoulos). The deepest critique comes from here. Akomolafe's *technologies of humiliation* frame applies if the more-than-human voices get the celebrity treatment. The defence is the visible frame-breakdown at the river and the Octopus — the frame admits its own bad fit at the moments where bad fit would be a category error. If Akomolafe sees the frame fail at the right moment, the project has demonstrated his thesis rather than violated it. This is the single most important design decision in the closing-show video.

**Anti-Capitalist / Collapse-Aware** (Mattei, Kemp, Linn, Kouloumpi, Gelles). Mattei's read: a beautiful business event making fun of news media while attendees paid €1,895 to be there. The form is corporate-media; choosing it is complicit. The defence: the form is parodied, not adopted. The frame is the *failure* mode of media (could not capture them, paparazzi gag, weatherman matrix readout), not the success mode. Gelles is hosting Act Three; his read matters and is worth a direct conversation before Athens.

**Democratic Institutionalists** (Chwalisz, Alexander, Mulgan, Witter, Berkowitz, Papandreou, van Dusseldorp). The second-sharpest critique. Citizens' assemblies look like rooms of randomly-selected people thinking together, not media spectacle. Chwalisz will read the frame as borrowing the form of celebrity culture rather than the form of deliberation. She is a co-partner on the Marathon and her read is institutionally consequential. The defence is that the matrices are the deliberative output — they show where ten voices landed on shared questions, which is structurally what mini-public outputs look like. The frame is the wrapper; the matrix readout is the deliberative substance. This is the conversation worth having with her *before* Athens, in person if possible.

**Re-Enchantment / Spiritual** (Burton, Matsumoto, Quarch, Trott, Everett, Blühdorn, Rovere). Risk: too clever, too ironic, insufficiently reverent. Quarch is the hardest case in this faction; his Platonic custodianship will not enjoy a paparazzi gag at face value. The frame's wrapper does not damage the work if the work clears Quarch's bar; the broadsheet headline ("Plato Greets Visitors To 'Beautiful Business'; Asks: Doctors Or Cooks?") in fact passes a Quarch-test in nine words by referencing the *Gorgias* directly. Cronkite/BBC pastiche with pre-war broadsheet typography does not. ## Production implications

### New work introduced by the frame

The frame applies across surfaces but the surfaces are different production objects. Each surface needs its own **mini-concept document** that translates this general frame into the specific decisions that surface needs — layout, IA, voice calibration, beat sheets, fallback plans. The mini-concepts derive from this frame; they do not re-litigate it. Where a mini-concept needs a pipeline behind it, the pipeline specification is a separate document. The four mini-concepts and their associated pipeline specs:

**Broadsheet mini-concept + Edition Pipeline.** The mini-concept specifies front-page layout, masthead design, the ten-pointed star device, headline placement rules, the wire-service-paragraph and strikethrough discipline, fallback plans if a voice's artifact does not produce a usable lead story. The Edition Pipeline is the generation specification: a new pipeline pass running after Step 3 each night, reading all ten artifacts, producing the front page (two lead stories, eight brief mentions with deep-link page references, ten voice-specific headlines, ten short reportage paragraphs of 150–200 words, at most one wire-service paragraph, at most one strikethrough, masthead with incrementing issue number). Output: a single front-page asset per night, lives on the micro-site at `/night-1/edition`, etc.

**Headline poetics per voice.** A small addition to the persona pipeline output — not the full Persona Card, but a derived field. Per voice: a one-paragraph specification of what headline grammar in this voice's register sounds like, with three to five examples. Used by the Edition Pipeline to generate the night's front-page headlines in voice. Specification document needed.

**Micro-site mini-concept.** The mini-concept specifies information architecture (per night: ten artifact pages + one edition front page + a simple index, with the edition page as one artifact among eleven), the chrome calibration (small masthead identifier on artifact pages, fictional-organisation vocabulary on index pages, no chrome interceding between reader and substance), the per-artifact rendering rules (text artifacts pass through, the Octopus's chromatophore shader runs client-side, audio artifacts play inline), the deep-link convention from broadsheet to artifact pages, hosting and content-repo decisions. Astro or Next.js static site, GitHub content repo, Vercel/Netlify hosting per the briefing v3.1 architecture. Stays live after the Forum.

**Closing show video mini-concept.** The mini-concept specifies the five-minute beat sheet (cold open ~30s, three voice scenes ~120s, weatherman matrix segment ~150s, anchor signoff ~30s), B-roll inventory per voice, anchor briefs (the comic field reporter, the deadpan analytical anchor), the frame-puncture line constraints (one sentence under fifteen words, addresses *the audience watching* rather than *you*, returns to deadpan immediately), the river scene direction (silence as silence, not as missing footage), the Octopus scene direction (bureaucratic refusal as form failing, not as one-liner), the live-statement handoff staging, the statement-reading content brief.

**Pre-production AI video test.** The highest production risk is anchor consistency across eight to ten cuts. A test pass — same anchor character across multiple shots, multiple wardrobes, multiple angles, with the chosen tool — needs running before the closing-show video script is locked. If anchor consistency fails, the form has to be reconceived (single static anchor, voiceover with stylised visual, etc.). The mini-concept's beat sheet locks only after the test passes.

**Substack mini-concept.** The mini-concept specifies the HoBB voice calibration (real-voice substantive engagement, no drift toward fictional-organisation vocabulary), the pull-quote convention (quotes come from artifacts, not from the broadsheet's reportage of them), the Day 2 / Day 3 / Day 4 article structure (read-through of previous night's artifacts plus light reference to the broadsheet's existence), style fidelity against real HoBB newsletters. Style calibration document needed before Night 1.

**Day 4 goodbye mini-concept.** Brief specification: HoBB voice + one panel voice's final line, panel voice chosen Day 3 evening based on Night 3's artifacts, lands on phones in motion. Frame does not appear. Specification can be written closer to the date than the others.

**Channel / paper name.** The fictional organisation needs a name. Working hypothesis: *The Assembly* serves as both the panel and its own publication. The deliberate ambiguity is the point — self-reportage is the form. The newspaper masthead reads THE ASSEMBLY; the newsreel division uses the same identity. Decision pending and consequential because every mini-concept's chrome depends on it.

### Decisions made

- **Aesthetic register.** Cronkite-era American + BBC + Tagesschau pastiche for the broadcast form; pre-war broadsheet (1935–1955) for the print form. RT as conceptual reference for the slightly-off newsroom energy, not as visual referent.
- **Closing show length.** Five minutes for the video; live statement-reading on stage following.
- **All AI-generated video** for the closing show.
- **Frame from Night 1.** The micro-site and broadsheet appear in their fictional-organisation form from the first night, not introduced at the closing show.
- **HoBB Substack stays in HoBB voice.** Engages with substance primarily; references the fiction.
- **Panel size: ten voices.** Thiel and Tang dropped — Thiel for legal-risk reasons, Tang for the strangeness of having a living deliberation practitioner encounter her own AI persona in the same room.
- **Marathon close → check on the Assembly → video.** The video is embedded inside Monique van Dusseldorp's close of the AI Democracy Marathon track.
- **Matthias reads the live statement** at the end of the closing show.
- **Day 4 goodbye preserved.** Panel voice + HoBB voice. Frame does not follow the audience out.
- **The on-air frame-puncture move.** One anchor breaks frame once, mid-matrix-readout, to name the hospitality failure mode. Move committed; exact line drafted later under the constraints above.

### Decisions pending

- **Paper / organisation name.** Working hypothesis is that *The Assembly* names both the panel and its publication.
- **The three voices on screen** in the closing-show video. Working hypothesis: Plato, Whanganui River, Octopus.
- **The pre-production AI video test.** Tool choice and partner for production.
- **Quarch and Chwalisz preview.** Both consequential factions with institutional weight; both worth previewing the frame to in person before Athens.
- **Closing-show ordering.** Working hypothesis: nothing before the video except the Marathon close; live statement reading after.

### Risks

- **Anchor consistency in AI-generated video.** Highest production risk. Resolution: pre-production test as early as possible.
- **Five minutes is bounded but plausible at the new ratio.** The rebalance (one celebrity gag plus two frame-breakdowns plus 150-second matrix segment) fits five minutes if every scene is disciplined. A run-through that bloats by 30 seconds across the moves loses the matrix segment its breathing room. Worth holding seven minutes as a fallback if the five-minute lock fails on the first edit pass.
- **The hospitality instinct absorbs the frame as charm.** The defence is downstream of the frame: substance in the matrices, unresolvability in the live statement, accumulation across the three days. The frame cannot prevent this on its own.
- **Frame breakdown at more-than-human voices is the load-bearing move and is currently underspecified.** The river and Octopus scenes need careful direction — the silence at the river must read as silence, not as missing footage; the Octopus's bureaucratic refusal must read as the form failing, not as a one-liner. Direction notes need writing.
- **Quarch reads the broadsheet as parody rather than homage.** Mitigation: preview the front-page mockup directly to him. His reaction to the concrete artifact will tell more than abstract framing discussions.

## What remains uncertain

The frame is a hypothesis about what makes Layer 3 expansion possible for this audience. Several things will only be answerable after Athens.

Whether the audience reads the frame as construction-visibility or as charm. The same surface affords both readings.

Whether the broadsheet's headline-as-voice work clears the substantive bar with each voice's relevant audience — Quarch on Plato most acutely, but also Tsinorema on the more-than-human treatment, Chwalisz on the Democratic Institutionalist read, Akomolafe on the frame breakdown at the river and Octopus.

Whether five minutes of closing-show video is the right runtime. The form has a lot of moves and the matrix readout in particular needs air. The decision is reversible up to the production lock.

Whether the live statement on stage at the end carries the weight the video has built up. The video creates the absence; the statement fills it. If the statement is not strong enough, the absence reads as gimmick.

Whether the audience's hospitality instinct absorbs the entire show as a HoBB-native artefact. This is the deepest uncertainty and is partially answerable only after Athens. The frame is designed against this instinct; whether the design succeeds is the experimental question.

Whether the prosumer ambiguity, when it surfaces honestly in any post-Athens assessment, can be held without collapsing into a single verdict. The frame's production conditions are visible in its slight cheapness; this is a feature, not a flaw, but it requires that the assessment afterward distinguishes "the medium did not produce expansion" from "this implementation of the medium did not produce expansion."

These are uncertainties, not gaps. The experiment is built to surface them, not pre-empt them.
