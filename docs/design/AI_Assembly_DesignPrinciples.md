**1. Deliberation, not ecology**

The default visual vocabulary for "more-than-human" is nature photography, watercolour botanicals, satellite imagery, forest light. All of it triggers the aesthetic-encounter frame and activates hospitality. Your visual language needs to read as *deliberative body* — closer to parliament, court record, assembly minutes, debate transcript — than as nature magazine. Think typographic, archival, documentary. When a river appears visually, it should appear the way a *position* appears, not the way a river appears.

The handle this generates: the Assembly looks like a record of a meeting, not a curated exhibition.

**2. The matrix is the primary metaphor, not a closing-show artifact**

The two matrices are the project's sharpest visual thinking. They do exactly what the project needs: they make disagreement legible, they implicate the viewer (where would *you* plot?), and they refuse the curated-voices frame because a matrix is about relations, not individuals. Promote them from closing-show payoff to *governing visual motif* across all surfaces. Every Substack read-through shows a mini-matrix. The micro-site navigates by matrix position, not by voice. The daily newsletter blurb has a tiny matrix fragment.

The handle this generates: the audience learns to read the Assembly spatially — as a landscape of positions — before the closing show arrives. By Day 3 evening, the matrices feel inevitable rather than revelatory.

**3. Friction-first composition**

Lead every visual with a collision, not a voice. The Substack's opening image is not Plato's portrait; it's Plato-and-the-Octopus on the same provocation, visibly at odds. The micro-site landing page is not a grid of twelve voices; it's a disagreement from last night, with the two voices named beneath. The closing show opens on an empty quadrant, not on a populated one.

The handle this generates: the audience's first visual encounter always contains tension, which means they have something to resolve — which means they cannot be hospitably neutral.

**4. Visible construction, not hidden craft**

The briefing is explicit: every layer of translation should be visible. Your visual language should make this an aesthetic choice, not a disclosure. Show the prompt alongside the artifact. Show the Researcher's theme alongside the Provocateur's formulation. Show the first draft alongside the amended draft, with the amendment visibly marked. Treat the construction as the material, the way Brutalist architecture treats concrete or the way a Godard film treats the edit.

The handle this generates: the audience cannot settle into "is this really the river's voice?" because the seams are the aesthetic. The construction question gets answered at the level of visual grammar before it can be asked in words.

**5. Typography does the heavy lifting**

Imagery is where the hospitality instinct finds purchase most easily — a beautiful image of a river is exactly the frame you're trying to defeat. Typography resists this. A voice rendered as text, in a specific typographic treatment, cannot be aestheticised the same way. Different voices can have different typographic registers (Plato in a dialogue setting, Arendt in essay register, the River in something spatial and interrupted, Marley in something rhythmic). The visual differentiation is typographic, not illustrative.

The handle this generates: the Assembly reads as a book of positions, not a gallery of portraits. The voices are distinguishable by how they *compose on the page*, not by icons or avatars representing them.

**6. One defensive choice: everyone in the same uniform**

The closing-show video has the instinct exactly right — sweatshirts and running shoes defeat the cognitive-warfare critique because a river in sweatpants cannot be read as pretentious. Extend this principle across the visual system. The twelve voices share a visual container — the same card format, the same typographic frame, the same matrix plotting conventions. Individual voice-expression happens *inside* a shared container that refuses the curated-gallery frame.

The handle this generates: the visual system reads as "twelve participants in one body" rather than "twelve curated perspectives." The Assembly is a *body*, visually, before the audience decides what kind of body it is.

**7. Specificity over atmosphere**

Every visual decision should pass the test: does this make the audience *more specific* about what's being said, or does it make them *more atmospheric*? Atmosphere is hospitality fuel. Specificity breaks it. Caption-style labels, precise attributions, explicit disagreement markers, quoted fragments with exact source — all of these force the audience into specificity. Mood, light, colour-as-feeling, ambient imagery — all of these feed atmosphere. When in doubt, choose the forensic over the evocative.

The handle this generates: the visual language feels closer to a court exhibit than to a magazine spread. The audience is being asked to assess, not to feel.

**8. The empty quadrant is a visual statement**

One of the matrix's sharpest moves is showing where *no voice went*. Empty quadrants are more provocative than populated ones — they ask why the Assembly collectively avoided a position, what would it mean to occupy it, who would. Your visual language should treat emptiness as a design element, not as something to fill. White space in the layout, unanswered questions at the end of artifacts, matrices with visible gaps, a closing show that ends on a quadrant nobody entered.

The handle this generates: the Assembly's output feels incomplete by design, which makes the audience the ones who have to complete it — which is Layer 3 expansion in its most literal form.

**9. The overnight gap shows up visually**

The architectural principle that humans deliberate by day and non-humans by night is not just operational — it's a visual opportunity. Night-state artifacts and day-state artifacts can be visually distinct. Timestamps become part of the grammar. The Substack read-through can show "what happened while you slept" as a genuinely temporal claim, not a metaphor. The micro-site can make the three nights navigable as distinct states.

The handle this generates: the audience experiences the Assembly as *temporal* rather than static — as something that happened, is happening, will happen — rather than as a fixed set of artifacts.

**10. Refuse the AI visual vocabulary**

Glowing gradients, neural-network visualisations, node-and-edge graphs, particle effects, anything that signals "this was made by AI" is fatal. It activates the AI-as-author frame and lets the audience be hospitable to a tool. The visual language should signal *assembly, record, document, deliberation* — not *machine intelligence, emergence, generative system*. If someone looking at a Substack read-through cold could guess this is about AI before they read the text, you've lost.

The handle this generates: the medium stays invisible. The audience encounters positions and disagreements, not an AI demo. When the AI question arises, it arises at the level of the construction-visible principle, on your terms.

------

**The single sentence test**

If a visual decision makes the audience want to say "that's beautiful," it is probably wrong. If it makes them want to say "wait, what did the Octopus actually mean by that," it is probably right. Beauty is where hospitality lives. Specific, answerable, contested questions are where expansion lives.
