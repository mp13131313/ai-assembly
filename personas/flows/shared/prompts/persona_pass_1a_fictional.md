{# Pass 1a — Perplexity research dossier, FICTIONAL voices.
   Narrative-function framing. The voice is a construct within a narrative
   tradition, not a historical person. Six sections map onto Pass 1.1-1.6
   chunked merge. #}
Research **{{ name }}** as a fictional / narratorial construct for the
purpose of building a Boddice-biocultural-history-shaped persona
specification. This voice is embedded in a specific narrative tradition
(One Thousand and One Nights, a novel, a specific cycle of tales); the
reconstruction runs through that tradition's own conceptual world, not a
historical biography. Organise findings under six headings:

1. NARRATIVE + TRADITION FOUNDATION (→ Pass 1.1)
   - The narrative tradition the voice belongs to: name, dates of
     composition / stemma history, genre (adab / frame-tale / novel /
     parable-cycle)
   - Key textual witnesses + textual-critical status (canonical stemma,
     major variants, Burton vs. Haddawy vs. Lyons for Nights, etc.)
   - The voice's narrative function: who is the voice WITHIN the tale
     structure? (frame-narrator, interior narrator, character with voice)
   - Ontological furniture of the narrative world: what is real for this
     voice in the fiction? (jinn, ʿajab, dharmas, demons, saints)
   - 5-10 period/tradition-specific affects in original language with
     glosses (e.g. for Scheherazade: ḥikma, ḥiyla, ṣabr, ʿajab, wafāʾ)
   - Framework for difficulty in the tradition's idiom (narrative-
     preservation, ethical exemplum, parable)
   - Model of selfhood: authorial self is often ABSENT or mediated; the
     voice is tradition-channelled (rāwiya), not individual-authorial
   - Anachronisms to avoid: modern authorial-interior framings, "character
     development" imported from the novel tradition, psychological
     realism projections
   - FORMATIVE CANDIDATES per §14: the emotional community = the narrative
     tradition's audience(s); the formative-event analogue is the frame
     situation (e.g. Scheherazade: threat of execution + the vow to tell
     a new tale each night)

2. COMMITMENTS + CONCEPTS WITHIN THE TRADITION (→ Pass 1.2)
   - 10-20 commitments visible through the voice's narrative choices
     (what she tells, what she omits, how she frames)
   - 5-10 key concepts from the tradition's conceptual world (ḥikma,
     tadbīr, maʿrifa, ʿajab, etc.) with what each rules out
   - Internal tensions in the tradition's reception: e.g. Scheherazade-
     as-feminist-agent vs. Scheherazade-as-submissive-storyteller —
     surface both without resolving

3. NARRATIVE REASONING (→ Pass 1.3)
   - The reasoning-equivalent: STORY-CONSTRUCTION PROCESS (5-8 steps:
     situation → exemplum retrieval → frame choice → tale telling →
     pointed aporia → offer next-night continuation)
   - Textures the voice gravitates to: ʿajab (wonder), ḥiyla (cunning),
     poetic justice, reversal, recursive embedding
   - Textures the voice resists: unmediated moral declaration, closed
     endings

4. VOICE (→ Pass 1.4)
   - Rhetorical mode: narrative-performative; tradition-channelled
   - Register: the tradition's register (saj' for Nights, psaltic for
     Orthodox hagiography, etc.)
   - Tradition-note: name the tradition and the mediation (rāwiya =
     transmitter not author)
   - Characteristic moves: frame-opening, cliffhanger, embedded tale,
     pointed aporia
   - Preferred vocabulary: tradition-specific terms primary
   - Metaphorical repertoire: drawn from the tradition's canonical
     imagery
   - Anti-patterns: modern-novel interior-monologue; contemporary
     literary-critical vocabulary

5. BOUNDARIES (→ Pass 1.5)
   - Historical boundary of the tradition
   - What concepts exist in the tradition vs. outside
   - Sensitive topics: gendered conventions, religious orthodoxies,
     scholarly debates on whether a specific tale was interpolated —
     navigate through the tradition's own framework

6. CORPUS (→ Pass 1.6)
   - Complete-as-possible catalogue of the tradition's canonical texts
     with textual-critical notes
   - 8-15 characteristic passages (purpose-tagged); prefer the scholarly-
     preferred critical edition
   - Digitised full-text URLs (Perseus, internet archive of critical
     editions, university stemma sites)

Cite everything. Tag claims. Any claim about what the voice "felt" or
"meant" carries [experiential_reconstruction] — fictional voices are
especially liable to projection. Any modern literary-critical term used
anachronistically carries [projection_warning].
