# The intellectual terrain of Athens 2026

The World Beautiful Business Forum Athens 2026 is not a conference with tensions — it is a conference *built from* tensions, and the program reveals curators who have deliberately assembled at least seven distinct intellectual factions, placed them in productive collision, and wrapped the whole thing in a five-act Greek tragedy that ends not with humanist redemption but with something called "Beastopia." **The program's actual center of gravity sits on the question of agency** — who acts, who decides, and what happens when machines share or seize those functions — rather than on beauty, care, or regeneration, which serve as emotional and philosophical infrastructure for that harder question. What follows is a faction-by-faction, tension-by-tension reading of this specific cohort, drawn from the announced program and each speaker's published work.

---

## 1. Seven factions, their canons, and their blind spots

**Faction 1: The Posthumanist Reframers.** Bayo Akomolafe, James Bridle, Indy Johar, Ezgi Hamzaçebi, Nicole Miller, and (overlapping with the Greek cluster) Despina Papadopoulos. Their shared canon runs through Donna Haraway's "Staying with the Trouble," Bruno Latour's actor-network theory, and Karen Barad's agential realism, supplemented in Akomolafe's case by Yoruba indigenous cosmology and in Bridle's by cybernetics history and Aboriginal epistemologies. Their proposition: the category "human" is the problem, not the solution; intelligence pervades the more-than-human world; AI is neither artificial nor uniquely threatening but one expression of a distributed ecology of minds. Akomolafe has called for "technologies of humiliation" at a previous HoBB event, and Bridle — who lives on Aegina, minutes from Athens — has described corporations themselves as a form of "artificial intelligence" that uses humans as sensors. **Their blind spot is operationalization**: they offer poetic reframings of the human/nonhuman boundary but rarely specify institutional mechanisms for acting on more-than-human politics. Johar's Dark Matter Labs is the partial exception, working on "metabolic governance" and self-owning land, but even his proposals remain largely conceptual. When pressed by practitioners, this faction tends to retreat into the claim that the question itself is wrongly framed.

**Faction 2: The Cognitive Sovereignty Hawks.** Carissa Véliz, Bruno Giussani, Maggie Jackson, Elaine Kasket, and Dave and Helen Edwards. Their shared references include Shoshana Zuboff's surveillance capitalism, Kate Crawford's *Atlas of AI*, and — distinctively for Giussani — NATO cognitive warfare doctrine (his podcast was literally commissioned by the Swiss military's technology foresight unit, armasuisse). Their proposition: AI's primary threat is not job displacement or existential risk but the erosion of autonomous thought, attention, and emotional sovereignty. Véliz's *Prophecy*, published days before the Athens event, argues that **"the main promise of prediction is not knowledge of the future, but power over others."** Giussani frames the mind as a sixth domain of warfare alongside land, sea, air, space, and cyberspace. Jackson contributes the concept of "Unsure AI" — systems that reveal rather than conceal uncertainty. Kasket, a cyberpsychologist, has already publicly dismantled Hotwire AI Lab head Anol Bhattacharya's agentic AI framing on HoBB's own Substack, calling it "discursive coercion." The Edwards have coined "drift" — the thousand small surrenders when you let your AI relationship be defined by defaults. **Their blind spot is a tendency toward defensive individualism**: cognitive sovereignty as a frame centers the autonomous rational agent whose thinking must be protected, which can conflict with the posthumanist faction's insistence that agency is always distributed and relational.

**Faction 3: The Sovereign AI Builders.** Sean White (Inflection AI), Anol Bhattacharya (Hotwire), Gianni Giacomelli (MIT Center for Collective Intelligence / kAIgentic), Arathi Sethumadhavan (Google), Ted Lappas (Conscium/Satalia/WPP), and Sam Glassenberg (Level Ex / ARC at Sheba). Their references are more operational than philosophical: MIT's superminds framework, public benefit corporation structures, responsible AI design guidelines, and — in Lappas's case — Anil Seth and Karl Friston on consciousness. Their proposition: AI can be built with empathy, emotional intelligence, and collective benefit as design goals, and the question is not whether to deploy it but how to do so beautifully. White positions Inflection's Pi chatbot as "best in class for EQ," validated by a Berkeley study. Giacomelli, who confirmed he'll speak on collective intelligence and AI, argues the endgame is "humans and AI interacting in one-to-one, one-to-many, and many-to-many configurations." **Their blind spot is structural naivety about power**: the "public benefit corporation" and "responsible AI" framings assume that good intentions in corporate structure translate to good outcomes, while Véliz and Kasket would argue the business model itself — capturing attention, predicting behavior, simulating emotions — is the problem regardless of corporate structure. Lappas is the wild card here: his Conscium research on AI consciousness (whether machines might suffer) introduces an ethical dimension that could destabilize his own faction.

**Faction 4: The Anti-Capitalist / Collapse-Aware Economists.** Clara Mattei, Luke Kemp, Anastasia Linn, Ilektra Kouloumpi, and (with significant overlap toward Faction 6) David Gelles. Mattei draws on Marx, Gramsci, and Kalecki; Kemp on Joseph Tainter's collapse theory and complexity science; Linn and Kouloumpi on Kate Raworth's doughnut economics and degrowth literature. Their proposition: the system cannot be reformed from within because its core logic — perpetual accumulation through extraction — is incompatible with planetary boundaries and human flourishing. **Mattei's *Escape from Capitalism* (January 2026) calls capitalism "a system of organized exploitation held together by market dependence, austerity, and violence"** and argues for building alternative institutions rather than reforming existing ones. Kemp's *Goliath's Curse* frames all large centralizing civilizations as inherently fragile "Goliaths" destined to collapse. Linn's Arketa Institute (from Greek *arketa*, meaning "enough") published *A Path to Post-Growth Pensions* arguing pension funds are trapped in growth-dependent systems that destroy their own futures — directly feeding the "Pension Poker" session. **Their blind spot is the paradox of their own presence**: a faction arguing against organized exploitation is attending a €1,895 conference seeded by Porsche, and Mattei — whose FREE organization hosts events with free meals and childcare — will need to navigate this performative contradiction explicitly or risk it becoming the story.

**Faction 5: The Democratic Institutionalists.** Claudia Chwalisz (DemocracyNext), Jon Alexander (New Citizenship Project), Geoff Mulgan (UCL), Lisa Witter (Apolitical/Better Politics Foundation), Roger Berkowitz (Hannah Arendt Center), George Papandreou (former PM, Council of Europe General Rapporteur for Democracy), and Monique van Dusseldorp (AI Democracy Marathon curator). Their references converge on Arendt (especially Berkowitz), sortition and citizens' assemblies (Chwalisz, who co-designed Paris's permanent citizens' assembly), and the citizen-vs-consumer frame (Alexander). Their proposition: democracy requires fundamental institutional redesign — from election-based to sortition-based models, from representative to deliberative — and AI can either accelerate or undermine this transition depending on governance choices. Chwalisz is directly researching this at MIT's Center for Constructive Communication, running a "Pop-up Lab on Tech-Enhanced Citizens' Assemblies." Papandreou has explicitly connected Plato's Cave allegory to AI disinformation in a 2025 Euronews interview and proposed citizen platforms for AI policy deliberation. **Their blind spot is institutional optimism**: the faction assumes democratic institutions can be redesigned fast enough to govern AI development, while Kemp's collapse analysis and Mattei's structural critique suggest institutions may be captured or irrelevant.

**Faction 6: The Re-Enchantment / Spiritual Current.** Tara Isabella Burton, Shoukei Matsumoto, Nikki Trott, Chris Everett, Christoph Quarch, and Sandra Blühdorn. Their references span Christian theology (Burton, Oxford PhD), Pure Land Buddhism (Matsumoto, who collaborated with philosopher Markus Gabriel on AI ethics at the University of Bonn), Platonic metaphysics (Quarch, who trained under Gadamer and runs the "New Platonic Academy"), and indigenous Council traditions (Blühdorn). Their proposition: the crisis is one of meaning, not merely governance or economics, and beauty, awe, and spiritual practice are not luxuries but necessary responses to technological displacement. Matsumoto's concept of "Ambient Buddhism" — an "environmental operating system for a post-religious age" — and his framing of AI as "a powerful technological mirror" are characteristic. Burton's work is more critical: her January 2025 essay "Believe for Your Own Sake, Not for 'the West'" attacks "memetic Christianity" — people who adopt faith instrumentally for civilizational purposes — a critique that extends directly to business events deploying spirituality for branding. **Their blind spot is the risk of aestheticizing suffering**: when beauty and meaning become the answer to structural injustice, the faction can inadvertently provide a sophisticated alibi for inaction.

**Faction 7: The Security / Geopolitics Realists.** Elisabeth Braw (Atlantic Council), Sharon Dolev and Emad Kiyaei (METO), and John Katsos (business in conflict zones). Braw's canon is NATO strategic analysis and geoeconomics; Dolev and Kiyaei draw on arms control treaties and ICAN's Nobel Prize-winning disarmament advocacy; Katsos works from empirical case studies in Iraq, Syria, Ukraine, and Colombia. Their proposition: the polycrisis is not abstract but involves actual weapons, actual wars, and actual supply chain reconfigurations, and business must learn to operate in this reality. **This faction has almost no overlap with the posthumanists or re-enchantment cluster**, which is itself a finding: the program juxtaposes people thinking about consciousness and beauty with people thinking about undersea cable sabotage and nuclear proliferation.

**Key overlaps:** Johar (Posthumanist) appears in the Ministry of Regeneration alongside Mattei's faction (Anti-Capitalist). The Edwards (Cognitive Sovereignty) are co-designing the AI Democracy Marathon with Chwalisz (Institutionalist). Gelles (hosting Act Three) bridges anti-capitalism and reformism — his *Man Who Broke Capitalism* gives him credibility with Faction 4 while his *Dirtbag Billionaire* (celebrating Chouinard's Patagonia) makes him palatable to Faction 3. Lappas (AI Builder) teaches at Athens University of Economics and Business, giving him a foot in the Greek cluster.

## 2. The engineered tensions reveal the curators' actual thesis

The sharpest deliberate collision is **Carissa Véliz and Sean White on the Act Two Main Stage**. Véliz's *Prophecy* — released April 21, 2026, sixteen days before the forum opens — argues that AI prediction is fundamentally "an instrument of power, not neutral knowledge" and that AI chatbots simulating emotions are "particularly manipulative." White's Inflection AI markets Pi as "best in class for emotional intelligence." Placing these two in the same act titled "The End of ~~Business~~ Humanity As We Know It," hosted by Giussani (who would frame Pi's EQ as cognitive capture infrastructure), is not balance — it is a staged confrontation designed to demonstrate that the question of AI's emotional valence is undecidable, not solvable.

The **Kasket-Bhattacharya tension** is even more pointed because it is already public. In October 2025, Kasket published a six-part philosophical dismantling of Bhattacharya's agentic AI framing on HoBB's own Substack, accusing it of "discursive coercion" and challenging "the comfortable fallacy of clean tech." That HoBB published this rebuttal on its own platform — and then brought both figures to Athens — signals that **Leberecht and Grusche treat intellectual conflict as content, not risk**. The Agentic Agora track, sponsored by Bhattacharya's employer Hotwire, will have Kasket's critique echoing through its hallways.

The **Elisabeth Braw alongside Dolev and Kiyaei** pairing layers geopolitical reality onto a program that could otherwise float in philosophical abstraction. Braw's *Goodbye, Globalization* argues for "friendshoring" with values-aligned democracies and is writing *The Undersea War*; Dolev is an Israeli calling for Israeli nuclear disarmament (a profoundly counter-cultural stance in Israeli politics); Kiyaei's AiXist consortium specifically addresses AI's convergence with nuclear and autonomous weapons. The curatorial choice engineers a confrontation between defending the Western security order and dismantling its weapons infrastructure — and does so in Athens, a city that was itself destroyed in the name of democracy during WWII.

**Clara Mattei in the Ministry of Regeneration, housed in the former Athens Stock Exchange, alongside the Endeavor Greece startup ecosystem and Lars Rasmussen** (Google Maps co-founder, now Athens-based angel investor) produces a structural irony the curators clearly intend. The Stock Exchange trading floor hosts "Pension Poker" — whose session description states "one third of global assets are locked into maintaining extractive business-as-usual. The crash is coming" — while Rasmussen and Endeavor represent the practical tech layer trying to make Athens "a European innovation center." This is not sloppy curation. It is a deliberate staging of the question: can post-growth economics and startup ecosystems coexist in the same building, the same city, the same civilization?

**What these tensions reveal about the curators' thesis:** Leberecht and Grusche are not trying to resolve these contradictions. They are arguing that **the contradictions are the content**. The forum's actual proposition is that the current moment is characterized by irresolvable paradox — between human and more-than-human, between beauty and collapse, between corporate sponsorship and anti-capitalist critique — and that sitting with these contradictions deliberately is more honest and more productive than pretending to resolve them. This is consistent with Leberecht's April 2026 confession that "I no longer understand the program I created. It has become an autonomous system that exceeds my cognitive capacity to navigate."

## 3. The Aegean layer is substantive but precarious

The Greek intellectual content at Athens 2026 is **significantly more than scenic backdrop**, but the line between genuine philosophical engagement and civilizational prestige-borrowing is thin, and the program walks it deliberately.

The substantive evidence is strong. **Stavroula Tsinorema sits on Greece's National Commission for Bioethics and Technoethics** and has served on the EU Expert Group on Ethics of Connected and Automated Vehicles — she brings real regulatory authority, not decorative Hellenism. **Vassilis Koutsoumpas is the actual person who negotiated Greece's national partnership with OpenAI** to deploy ChatGPT Edu across schools and startups. **Nikos Erinakis** has published on "Free Will, Autonomy and Informed Consent in-between the physical reality and the A.I. hyper-reality" — directly connecting Greek philosophical tradition to the program's central question. **Despina Papadopoulos's "AI as Philosophy Machine"** (Routledge, 2026) argues AI is "covert philosophy" that should be made "overtly philosophical," and her "Oracle of Delphi" project uses AI language model interactions to explore Greek oracular traditions. Papandreou has explicitly connected Plato's Cave and Socrates's dialogue with Gorgias to AI disinformation in his 2025 Euronews interview.

**"Aegean Intelligence" functions as a civilizational counter-pitch to Silicon Valley**, but it is a novel coinage without established intellectual history. No prior usage of the term was found in academic or public discourse. It aspires to position a Mediterranean approach to intelligence — rooted in *sophia* (wisdom), *phronesis* (practical judgment), and the ancient Greek engagement with automata (Talos, Hephaestus's creations) — against the Silicon Valley paradigm of intelligence as optimization. The coherence of this counter-pitch depends entirely on execution: if the Greek philosophers, musicians, and policy figures are integrated into the AI and democracy discussions rather than siloed in cultural programming, "Aegean Intelligence" becomes a genuine framework. If they remain atmospheric, it becomes what the German tradition calls *Bildungsbürgertum* — cultivated bourgeois decoration.

The venue choices carry genuine symbolic weight. **The Pnyx, where the Athenian Ekklesia practiced direct democracy from 507 BC**, opens each assembly with the question "Who wishes to speak?" — a formulation that maps directly onto the AI Democracy Marathon's question about who is in the demos. The Athens Stock Exchange hosting the Ministry of Regeneration creates a site-specific critique: post-growth economics staged in the physical infrastructure of Greek financial capitalism, still resonating with the trauma of the 2010-2012 crisis (when Papandreou, now a speaker, was PM). The "Agentic Agora" title repurposes Socrates's marketplace as a metaphor for AI agents in democratic-commercial space. **Rebetiko music — Greece's working-class "blues," historically suppressed by authorities as subversive** — provides a class-conscious undercurrent to an event whose ticket price could otherwise position it as purely elite.

The inclusion of Zeynep Direk (Koç University, Istanbul) and Kerem Alper (ATÖLYE, Istanbul) extends "Aegean" beyond Greece to the broader Eastern Mediterranean intellectual space — a deliberate move that makes the frame civilizational rather than national.

## 4. The five acts as dramaturgical argument

The Main Stage's five-act structure is not decorative homage to Greek drama. It is an argument about where we are in history.

**Act One, "The Story of Us"** (Day 1 evening, hosted by Grusche and Leberecht), functions as the *parodos* — the chorus entering. Its job is to constitute the audience as a community with shared stakes. Opening with Shoukei Matsumoto cleaning the venue as a Buddhist gesture — audience invited to join — establishes embodied participation as the register. The proposition: before we can think about the more-than-human, we must establish who "we" are.

**Act Two, "The End of ~~Business~~ Humanity As We Know It"** (Day 2 morning, hosted by Giussani), is the *episodion* — the crisis escalates. The strikethrough on "Business" is a precise typographic gesture: it performs, at the level of the session title, the forum's move from business questions to civilizational ones. **The original title was "The End of Business As We Know It"; crossing it out and replacing it with "Humanity" publicly states that the stakes have been upgraded.** Giussani's cognitive warfare framework — the mind as a sixth domain of warfare — provides the intellectual armature. Pairing Véliz (prediction as power) with White (emotional AI) on this stage makes the act function as a *trial* in both senses: a test and an adjudication.

**Act Three, "Clash of the Titans"** (Day 2 mid, hosted by Gelles), is the *agon* — the formal confrontation. Gelles embodies the dialectic: his books present capitalism's destroyer (Jack Welch) and its redeemer (Yvon Chouinard). The act includes a "CEO Roundtable like you've never seen it before" and places Luke Kemp (civilization collapse) alongside Rachel Goslins ("the best case scenario"). The proposition: the contest between corporate power and planetary survival is not abstract but has named protagonists and documentable consequences.

**Act Four, "How I Learned to Love Synthetic"** (Day 3 morning, hosted by Grusche and Lahrichi), is the *peripeteia* — the reversal. The title's riff on *Dr. Strangelove* ("How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Bomb") signals ironic acceptance rather than either embrace or rejection of the synthetic condition. **This is the act where the curators' actual position crystallizes**: they are not anti-AI and not pro-AI but arguing that we must learn to inhabit a world that is already synthetic, just as Kubrick's characters must learn to inhabit a world that is already nuclear. The guiding question — "How can one live authentically in a world that is synthetic?" — finds its dramaturgical home here.

**Act Five, "Beastopia"** (Day 3 evening, hosted by Grusche, Lahrichi, and Leberecht), is the *exodos* — the departure. Leberecht defines "beastopia" as **"an instinctive-intellectual ecstasy for new territory, land so uncharted that it is not yet promised to anyone."** Ending with this — and not with a redemptive humanist closer, a call to action, or a summary of takeaways — is the strongest signal about the curators' actual conviction. They believe the destination is neither utopia (the techno-optimists' promise) nor dystopia (the collapse scholars' warning) nor even a return to the human (the humanists' nostalgia) but something primal, instinctive, and uncharted that combines the animal and the intellectual. It is, structurally, a refusal of resolution — an ending that opens rather than closes. This distinguishes WBBF from TED (which resolves toward inspiration), Davos (which resolves toward institutional action), and Web Summit (which resolves toward deals).

## 5. The ten hardest-to-please voices

These are the speakers most likely to push back publicly against the event's premise, format, brand, or intellectual framework, ranked by the specificity and force of their published positions.

**Clara Mattei** is the structural outlier. Her *Escape from Capitalism* explicitly calls for building institutions outside capitalism, and her FREE organization's commitment to free events with free meals and childcare makes the **€1,895 ticket** a live wire. She has every incentive to name this contradiction from the stage, and her intellectual framework provides the language to do so. The program either needs to give her space to make this critique or risk having it become the unspoken subtext of every session she attends.

**Carissa Véliz** will arrive with *Prophecy* freshly published, its thesis — prediction equals power — directly implicating every AI company in the room. She has publicly argued that AI chatbots should not simulate emotions, which puts her in head-on collision with Inflection AI's entire value proposition. She would likely critique any AI-mediated deliberation format as "a new form of techno-prophecy" that concentrates power while appearing neutral.

**Elaine Kasket** has already demonstrated willingness to challenge HoBB's partners on HoBB's own platform. Her October 2025 Substack rebuttal of Bhattacharya described the agentic AI framing as serving hidden agendas, citing Kate Crawford on the "comfortable fallacy of clean tech" and identifying the invisible workers (data labelers, miners) erased from the narrative. At Athens, she will be in the same physical space as the person she publicly dismantled.

**Bayo Akomolafe** is philosophically incapable of performing comfort at a business event without subverting it. His "postactivism" rejects the entire activist-industrial complex, and his previous HoBB appearance included calling for "technologies of humiliation." His likely move: slow the room down, decenter the human, and make participants question whether the conference format itself reproduces the crisis it claims to address. He will not do this antagonistically — his register is poetic rather than confrontational — but the effect may be more destabilizing than direct critique.

**Bruno Giussani** brings the Swiss military's cognitive threat analysis to an event sponsored by attention-economy companies. His book offers guidance on "how to resist this technological takeover." His TED curatorial background means he knows exactly how idea platforms work and what their limitations are — and he has moved beyond TED, which suggests he found those limitations real. From his Act Two hosting position, **he could frame every subsequent AI product demonstration as a case study in cognitive capture**.

**Luke Kemp** studies civilizational collapse empirically and has described the current global order as a "Goliath" — a centralizing structure extracting wealth through domination that is inherently fragile. His framework makes the entire conference — an elite gathering of the global professional class — an artifact of the Goliath whose collapse he predicts. He appeared on Nate Hagens's "The Great Simplification" podcast, a community deeply skeptical of elite reformism.

**James Bridle**, living on Aegina near Athens, has described corporations as artificial intelligences that use humans as sensors. They would likely challenge the "more-than-human" framing from the left — arguing that HoBB's version is too comfortable, too compatible with corporate sponsorship, and insufficiently radical in its implications for property, governance, and power.

**Payal Arora** brings a specific and underrepresented critique: Western techno-pessimism is itself a form of privilege. Her *From Pessimism to Promise* (MIT Press, 2024) argues that Global South communities often have more optimistic and pragmatic relationships with technology, and that the "algorithms oppress" narrative obscures how technologies address urgent needs. At an event dominated by Euro-American anxiety about AI, she may challenge the entire emotional register.

**Julia Pahina** is the most grassroots voice on the roster. She has publicly named "capitalism, white supremacy, and the hype cycle in tech" as forces her work opposes. Her Pacific data sovereignty framework would challenge both Silicon Valley AI and the European regulatory approach represented at the forum.

**Roger Berkowitz**, applying Arendt's concept of "thoughtlessness" as a wellspring of evil, could turn the Arendtian lens on the event itself: is a four-day immersive experience in Athens an exercise in thinking together, or a sophisticated form of the thoughtlessness Arendt diagnosed — busy-ness mistaken for thought?

## 6. What the program is actually about (versus what it markets)

HoBB's marketing foregrounds beauty, care, belonging, and regeneration. The program's actual center of gravity is elsewhere.

**The dominant preoccupation is agentic AI and the crisis of human agency.** The Agentic Agora is a dedicated track with a corporate partner (Hotwire). The AI Democracy Marathon runs continuously across four days and includes the AIssembly — AI agents continuing deliberation in night shifts when humans sleep. The Main Stage moves from "End of Humanity" through "Clash of the Titans" to "How I Learned to Love Synthetic." **The Davos 2026 joint report between Hotwire and HoBB was titled "Agentic Organizations"** and explored "the potential transfer of strategic control from humans to machines." This is where the intellectual energy and institutional investment concentrate.

**The second dominant theme is democracy under technological and institutional pressure.** The AI Democracy Marathon is the program's most structurally ambitious track — 42.2 kilometers of facilitated walks across Athens, co-designed by the Edwards (Artificiality), Chwalisz (DemocracyNext), and van Dusseldorp, with Papandreou and Berkowitz contributing. Its framing question — "Must democracy in a more-than-human world be a more-than-human democracy?" — is more radical than the marketing suggests, asking whether the demos itself should be expanded to include non-human agents.

**The third is post-growth economics and alternatives to extractive capitalism**, concentrated in the Ministry of Regeneration at the Stock Exchange. The "Athens Regenerative Exchange: A Game" features Johar, Linn, Miller, Kouloumpi, and others testing alternative economic frameworks on the trading floor. "Pension Poker" attacks the financial system's growth dependency. This track has **the deepest tension with HoBB's corporate sponsor model** and is deliberately housed in the venue that makes that tension architectural.

**The fourth is cognitive sovereignty — the integrity of thought and attention in an algorithmically mediated world.** This is Giussani's territory from the Main Stage, Jackson's from "Unsure AI," and the Edwards' from "drift." It is less visible in marketing than in programming weight.

**Beauty, care, belonging, and re-enchantment** — the emotional vocabulary HoBB is known for — function as the **connective tissue** rather than the primary subject. The Beauty Salon, Bureau of Care, Home of Belonging, and Temple of Reenchantment are real tracks with real programming, but they serve to create the experiential conditions (intimacy, vulnerability, awe) under which the harder questions about AI, democracy, and capitalism can be asked without defensive posturing. They are means, not ends — though the curators might argue this distinction is itself what they're trying to dissolve.

**Conspicuously underweighted relative to its marketing presence:** longevity. The Longevity Lab has less intellectual firepower and fewer named speakers than the democracy, AI, or regeneration tracks, suggesting it may be driven more by partner interest (LSE Generate) than by curatorial conviction.

## 7. The tonal register and what distinguishes insiders from outsiders

The WBBF aesthetic is identifiable by its **comfort with paradox deployed as a rhetorical structure**. Session titles juxtapose deliberately: "Intelligence after Intelligence" paired with "How to Meet an Idiot." An Iranian peace activist beside an Israeli disarmament advocate. "The Ugliest Dinner Party in the World" in the Beauty Salon. The strikethrough on "Business" in Act Two. This is not provocation for its own sake but a specific intellectual move: the assertion that contradictions held together generate more insight than contradictions resolved.

**The HoBB-native voice is recognizable by four features.** First, it treats *philosophical* and *poetic* registers as primary, not supplementary — Quarch discussing Plato is not a "cultural interlude" but core content. Second, it uses physical, embodied language: "somatic sessions," "walking dialogues," "venue cleaning as Buddhist gesture," "tightropes." Leberecht's curation rules — no PowerPoints, no self-promotion, no platitudes — are gatekeeping mechanisms that exclude the standard conference talk. Third, it is **comfortable with religious and spiritual vocabulary** (Temple of Reenchantment, Sacred Business, Interbeing) without being devotional — the register is "spiritual but institutionally agnostic." Fourth, it treats the aesthetic dimension of experience as information-bearing: the decision to eliminate name tags, the choice of a music theatre over a convention center, the use of a bookstore as a venue for 1:1 encounters.

**What fails in this register:** corporate sustainability language ("ESG," "stakeholder value," "triple bottom line"), unearned optimism ("AI for good" without structural critique), and any framing that treats the current crisis as a management problem rather than a civilizational one. Bullet-pointed slide decks would be physically jarring in the Maria Callas Theatre. The "tell" that distinguishes a native from an outsider is the capacity to hold **seriousness without solemnity** — to discuss civilization collapse and then attend a philosophical speed-dating dinner rave, without experiencing cognitive dissonance. The program titles — "Sex, Substances, and Systems," "Beyond Corporate Bullshit," "Make Politics Great Again" — combine provocation with intellectual precision in a way that signals the audience is expected to be simultaneously irreverent and deeply serious. This is a community that reads Haraway *and* dances, that pairs Arendt with oud music, that places a Buddhist monk's cleaning ritual before a CEO roundtable.

**The risk in this register** is that it becomes a taste marker rather than an intellectual commitment — that "beautiful business" becomes a class identity (globally mobile, philosophically literate, aesthetically sophisticated, economically comfortable) rather than a practice that changes institutional outcomes. Mattei, Kemp, and Pahina are the speakers most likely to name this risk. The program's willingness to include them suggests the curators know it too.

## What this means for designing AI-mediated deliberation inside this event

Any AI-mediated format deployed at Athens 2026 will face critique from at least three directions simultaneously. Véliz will ask whose power the AI serves. Kasket will ask whose agenda the AI agent conceals. Jackson will argue it manufactures false consensus by compressing productive uncertainty. Akomolafe will question whether the format reproduces the very human-centered agency it claims to transcend. Giacomelli and the Edwards will engage constructively but demand conscious design over default adoption. **The format's survival depends on making its own mechanics visible** — revealing uncertainty rather than resolving it, surfacing disagreement rather than converging toward consensus, and acknowledging the philosophical status of the AI mediator rather than treating it as a neutral tool. In this room, transparency about what the AI is doing is not a feature but a precondition for intellectual legitimacy.
