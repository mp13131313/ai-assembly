# Reconstructing the world, not the feeling: Boddice's history of emotions for the Athens Persona Pipeline

**BLUF.** Rob Boddice's core move — that emotions are biocultural artefacts of worlds, not universal constants inside heads — should be absorbed into the Persona Card v2 by rewriting two fields and auditing four more. The `world` field must stop being period-setting and become the voice's *emotional-experiential ontology*: what was real, what could be felt, what gave suffering meaning, what counted as a self. The `formative_experience` field must drop its "core wound" framing — a 1980s Anglo-American therapeutic sediment documented by Fassin, Furedi, and Illouz — and become a *formative emotional community* description, usable for a river and an octopus as well as for Plato. Four adjacent fields (`character`, `voice_signature`, `relationships`, `dialogue_instincts`) silently import modern psychological categories and need period-specific vocabulary and warning tags. Of the twelve panel voices, the Octopus, Whanganui River, Plato, Ibn Battuta, Cleopatra, Scheherazade, Dostoevsky, and Marley are severely affected; Lovelace and Arendt are partially affected; Tang and Thiel are least affected but still require care. Draft field instructions with worked examples for each voice-type follow in §13–§15.

---

## 1. Boddice's theoretical framework: worlds make feelings, feelings make worlds

Boddice's foundational claim is that emotions are "the dynamic product of the existence of our minds and bodies in moments of time and space" (*A History of Feelings*, Reaktion 2019). Against what he calls "a still powerful psychological orthodoxy that emotions in humans are universal, automatic, non-cognitive, natural," he holds that **the concepts available to a person are formative of experience** — a thesis he takes up "from an increasingly culturally aware critical neuroscience." His slogan from *The History of Emotions* (Manchester UP 2018, p. 143): **"We are, in a profound sense, our worlds. We are biocultural."** The HEX Digital Handbook formulation is cleaner still: "Body-minds form worlds and worlds form body-minds in an ever-unfolding dynamic instability."

Evidence he marshals is forensic-microhistorical rather than aggregative: Achilles' *menis* (not "wrath"); Spinoza's *affectus* (not "emotions"); the eighteenth-century Carte de Tendre; Wollstonecraft's "be happy"; medieval pain mediated by Christ-like suffering; the vanished emotional category *Zärtlichkeit*. Across *Pain: A Very Short Introduction* (OUP 2017) and *Knowing Pain* (Polity 2023) he treats nociceptors as "harm receptors" — signals that only become pain through culturally validated meaning. The world writes the felt.

Crucially, this is not linguistic determinism. Boddice collaborates with neuroscience (the Smail-co-authored "Neurohistory" chapter in Burke & Tamm's *Debating New Approaches*, 2018; the "developing brain as historical artifact" article, *Developmental Psychology* 55, 2019) and treats the brain as plastically enculturated. Language is one thread in a biocultural web that also includes built environment, ritual, material culture, power, and body chemistry.

## 2. The HEX Centre and inferring experience from silent sources

The Academy of Finland Centre of Excellence in the History of Experiences (HEX) at Tampere, where Boddice was senior research fellow, reframes the object of study from *emotion* to *experience* — defined methodologically, not essentially. Its *Digital Handbook of the History of Experience* (2022) treats experience as "theoretically and methodologically conceptualized study of human experiencing," not as access to authentic inner life. Entries cover narrative construction (Eiranen), autobiographical memory (Pihko), children's voices (Olsen), and lived religion (Toivo, Peake).

The method infers experience by reconstructing **"contexts of possibility"** — the biocultural web delimiting what could be felt. Because experience leaves only indirect traces, HEX scholars triangulate:

- **Linguistic-conceptual** (what words were available, their semantic fields, period thesauri, lexicons of feeling);
- **Material-spatial** (the built environment, objects, architectures of ritual);
- **Embodied-practice** (gestures, postures, ceremonies, disciplines);
- **Political-theological** (what authority said could be felt, what regimes rewarded or punished).

Applied to a source that does not report experience directly — say, a cellar inventory, a court record, a medical prescription — the historian reconstructs what kinds of experience such a world *made possible* and *precluded*. The gap between source and experience is bridged not by empathy but by disciplined inference from context. This is the HEX signature move.

## 3. Emotions in the world, not in the head — and how that differs from Barrett

Boddice welcomes Lisa Feldman Barrett's *How Emotions Are Made* (2017) as an ally: her work has "unpicked many of the prevailing psychologizing tendencies, pointing to the remarkable plasticity of the human brain and the worldedness of synaptic development." But his position is **more externalist than hers**. Where Barrett locates construction primarily *inside the brain* (interoception + core affect + learned concepts = constructed emotion), Boddice pushes construction out into the world: "Neither the brain, viscera, the vagus nerve, nor homeostasis are automatic and autonomic to the extent that they account for meaningful experience without the mediation of 'the external' and the layers of embedded experience that come from formational time in a culture… Culture is not merely the gloss of biology. The two are wholly entangled" (HEX Handbook, "Authenticity and the Dynamics of Experience").

He is closer to Monique Scheer's practice-theoretical claim that **"emotion (or affect) and expression are not two things, but one process"** (*History & Theory* 51, 2012) than to Barrett's predictive-coding brain. The difference matters for the pipeline: if emotions live in brains, voicing a historical figure means simulating their neural conceptual repertoire; if emotions live in worlds, voicing them means reconstructing the ontology-ritual-language-body complex in which they were possible. The pipeline should follow Boddice's externalism — it is more tractable from sources and less speculative about vanished brains.

## 4. Neuroscience, with caveats

Boddice uses neuroscience tactically: he wants the *constructionist* strand (Barrett, Gendron, Russell, Fridlund, cultural psychiatry via Kirmayer) against the *essentialist* strand (Ekman, Panksepp, classical affect-programme theory). He tells historians they "ignore the neurosciences at our peril" (*History of Emotions* p. 34) but "stops short of exhorting historians to become competent neuroscientists themselves." His handling of the Sapir-Whorf-adjacent claim "words shape emotions" is **not** strong linguistic determinism but practice-theoretical: words are one component of emotional practice alongside bodies, objects, and rituals. Having the word *Zärtlichkeit* mattered; so did the furniture of drawing rooms, the correspondence conventions, and the sentimental-novel genre that cultivated it.

## 5. Critical reception — the strongest contestation

Reviewers praise Boddice's integration of Rosenwein's "emotional communities," Reddy's "emotional regimes," and Stearns's "emotionology" as a coordinated toolkit (Crane, *Social History of Medicine* 2019). The sharpest critique is **Barbara Rosenwein's review in the *American Historical Review*** (126:2, June 2021). She argues *A History of Feelings* "does not quite succeed": "To shun the word 'emotion,' Boddice resorts to more general terms like 'feeling' and 'affective life' as if that solves the problem. It does not. Although he spends much time scolding others for mistranslating 'feeling words' with modern terms, he himself is often compelled to commit the same sin." Thomas Dixon's 2019 Q&A pushes the same point: if Greek *orgē*, Latin *ira*, French *colère* are *not* "anger," why group them under the English master-category "anger"? Boddice concedes the danger "is ever-present" and defends the practice as productive undoing.

**Takeaway for the pipeline**: Rosenwein's critique is not a refutation but a warning — any reconstruction is contaminated by the reconstructor's own vocabulary. Hence Boddice's maxim that reconstruction is **"doubly biased"**: biased by the sources' own interpretive frames, and biased by the historian's inherited categories. The pipeline should operationalize this as an explicit evidence tag (see §10).

## 6. Key concepts for the pipeline

- **Passions vs. emotions.** Boddice relies on Thomas Dixon's *From Passions to Emotions* (CUP 2003): the modern category "emotion" was effectively invented c. 1820 by Thomas Brown at Edinburgh, displacing the older quadripartite vocabulary (appetites, passions, affections, sentiments) that was morally laden and integrated with cognition. Before 1820, people had passions — things that *befell* them — not emotions they "had."
- **Suffering as meaning-making.** Pain is "always experienced subjectively and contextually" (*Pain: A VSI*). Meaning-making — religious (Christ's suffering), gender, class, racial — produces felt quality. "No single and universally accepted meaning of pain" exists; cultures supply frameworks.
- **Down with empathy.** Empathy is a late-19th-century translation of *Einfühlung*, not a universal capacity. Boddice's HEX workshop "Experience without foundations, or the perils of empathy" articulates it. "There is nothing about my humanness that affords me insight into humanity" (Beckerman, *The Atlantic*, Jan 2026). Naive empathy projects modern feelings onto historical actors; what should replace it is disciplined reconstruction of contexts of possibility.
- **Reconstruction is doubly biased.** The phrase (concept verified, exact wording paraphrased) captures: reconstruction is biased by the limits and frames of surviving sources, *and* by the modern historian's own conceptual vocabulary, which infiltrates even the decision of what to study.

## 7. Method — concrete prescriptions

Drawn across the four books plus HEX and interview materials:

1. **Refuse "emotion" as a master category a priori.** Work with period-specific terms: *menis, cholos, passio, affectus, sentiment, tendre*. Treat English itself as a historical artefact.
2. **Read sources in original languages** wherever possible; modern translations smuggle universalist assumptions.
3. **Combine Reddy + Rosenwein + Stearns** — emotional regimes (power), emotional communities (local variation), emotionology (prescriptive norms) — as integrated epistemological reminders.
4. **Use "moral economy"** to link emotion, morality, and politics (Ch. 8, *History of Emotions*).
5. **Treat emotion and expression as one process** (Scheer): follow the *doing* of feeling — gesture, ritual, spatial practice.
6. **Integrate sense and emotion** (Boddice & Smith 2020); reconstruct "world-brain-body dynamic" events.
7. **Engage social neuroscience critically** — against essentialism, not in search of universals.
8. **Attend to material, spatial, architectural.** Built environment and objects constitute feeling.
9. **Forensic microhistory + episodic reconstruction.** Deliver defamiliarising case studies, not aggregative longue durée.
10. **Politicise the happiness industry.** Treat contemporary emotional metrics as ideological.
11. **Be reflexively critical of your own emotional vocabulary.** Ask: where does it come from? Who has a stake in shaping it?
12. **Abandon empathy as a research warrant**; replace with reconstruction of contexts of possibility.
13. **Decentre "emotion" in favour of "experience."** Experience is broader and includes sense, reason, and practice.

## 8. Positions in the field and where Boddice fits

The history of emotions field can be mapped on two axes: universal ↔ constructed, and linguistic ↔ embodied.

- **Rosenwein (*Emotional Communities*, 2006; *Generations of Feeling*, 2016).** Groups share particular values and modes of feeling; individuals belong to several overlapping communities. Method: close reading of emotion-words across diverse corpora. Criticised for under-theorising how emotions actually feel and for lacking a mechanism of change.
- **Reddy (*Navigation of Feeling*, 2001).** *Emotives* are first-person emotion utterances that are simultaneously descriptive and performative (stating "I am angry" alters the state). *Emotional regimes* are normative structures with official rituals and emotives; *emotional refuges* are spaces of relative freedom; *emotional liberty* is a cross-cultural political criterion. Criticised as too top-down, too state-centric, and for smuggling in Western liberal individualism.
- **Plamper (*History of Emotions: An Introduction*, 2015).** Maps the universalism/constructivism binary and tries to transcend it via neuroscience. Boddice criticises this as preserving the binary.
- **Stearns & Stearns, "Emotionology" (*AHR* 1985).** Studies emotional *standards* (what cultures prescribe) separately from emotional *experience* — a distinction Scheer and Boddice dissolve.
- **Frevert (*Emotions in History — Lost and Found*, 2011).** A "historical economy of emotions": emotions are gained and lost (acedia and honour fade; empathy and compassion are modern inventions). Gender as the primary naturaliser.
- **Ekman (basic emotions, from 1970s).** Six-seven universal, biologically hard-wired emotions with cross-cultural facial signatures (FACS, POFA). Central foil for the field. Gendron, Roberson & Barrett (*Emotion* 2014) replicated the Himba study and found no universal recognition; Siegel et al. (*Psychological Bulletin* 2018) found no autonomic fingerprints.
- **Strong social constructionism** (Harré 1986; Lutz 1988; Abu-Lughod 1986; M. Rosaldo 1980). Emotions are culturally learned; no stable biological substrate.
- **Barrett's theory of constructed emotion** (2017). Emotions constructed in the brain from interoception + core affect + learned concepts. Predictive, concept-dependent. Emotional granularity depends on vocabulary.
- **Scheer, "Emotions as a Kind of Practice"** (*History & Theory* 51, 2012). Bourdieu-influenced: emotions are *done*, not *had*. Four modes: mobilising, naming, communicating, regulating. Dissolves body/mind and expression/experience dichotomies.
- **Dixon (*From Passions to Emotions*, 2003).** "The emotions" as a secular psychological catch-all was invented c. 1820 in Scottish philosophical psychology.

**Boddice sits at the biocultural-practice corner**, closest to Scheer and Dixon, sympathetic to Barrett while pushing further externalist, furthest from Ekman's essentialism and from strong linguistic-only constructionism. He absorbs Rosenwein-Reddy-Stearns as tools rather than choosing among them.

## 9. Historically specific emotions — a catalogue for the pipeline

Emotions that existed in one period/culture but not another, or that shifted dramatically. Short, concrete. Sources inline.

- **Acedia.** 4th–14th C. Christian monasticism. Evagrius Ponticus *Praktikos*: "the noonday demon," attacking monks between the fourth and eighth hour, producing distaste for the cell, exaggerated concern for health, urge to abandon vocation. Evagrius: "a simultaneous, long-lasting movement of anger and desire, whereby the former is angry with what is at hand, while the latter yearns for what is not present." Cassian Latinised it as *acedia id est anxietas seu taedium cordis*. Gregory the Great dropped it from the sins; Aquinas reintegrated it and folded it toward *tristitia spiritualis*. Subsumed into "sloth" in the medieval seven-sins list — but this is a *loss of phenomenology*, not a synonym. Modern depression and burnout overlap symptomatically; the spiritual-cosmological *meaning* is gone.
- **Amae** (甘え). Japanese, theorised by Takeo Doi, *Amae no Kōzō* / *The Anatomy of Dependence* (1971, Eng. 1973). "The indulging, passive love which surrounds and supports the individual in a group"; "to depend and presume upon another's benevolence." Modelled on the infant-mother relationship, extended to adult Japanese social life (boss, senior colleague, spouse). English has no single word; "dependency" in Anglo-American psychology is pathologised where *amae* is a positive social glue. Critics have called Doi's framing nihonjinron, but the term's distinctiveness holds.
- **Saudade.** Portuguese. A "sweet and bitter" longing for what is past or distant, carrying hope it may return. Teixeira de Pascoaes built the 20th-C *Saudosismo* movement around it. Carolina Michaëlis de Vasconcelos notes similar but non-equivalent terms in other languages; fundamentally tied to Portuguese maritime-imperial displacement.
- **Toska** (тоска). Russian. Nabokov's canonical gloss: "At its deepest and most painful, it is a sensation of great spiritual anguish, often without any specific cause. At less morbid levels it is a dull ache of the soul, a longing with nothing to long for, a sick pining, a vague restlessness, mental throes, yearning. In particular cases it may be the desire for somebody or something specific, nostalgia, love-sickness. At the lowest level it grades into ennui, boredom." A single word for a family of graduated states. Central to Russian literary selfhood (Dostoevsky, Chekhov).
- **Sehnsucht.** German Romantic. A longing that *moves* — "Wenn die Sehnsucht größer ist als die Angst, wird der Mut geboren" ("when Sehnsucht is greater than fear, courage is born"). Unlike saudade, it propels action; unlike nostalgia, it aspires toward a transcendent object.
- **Zärtlichkeit / tenderness.** 18th–19th C. European. Boddice's own example: an overarching emotional category of sentimentalism — cultivated in correspondence, drawing-room encounters, Empfindsamkeit literature — that no longer exists as a lived experience. Not "affection" or "warmth"; a specific refined, sometimes erotic, sometimes devotional mode requiring the material culture (letters, tea-tables, couches) and gendered script to be felt at all.
- **Melancholia (humoral)** vs. **depression (modern).** Humoral melancholia was caused by black bile, embodied in body-chemistry, valorised as the temperament of genius (Aristotle's *Problemata* 30.1; Burton's *Anatomy of Melancholy*, 1621); depression is a neurochemical disorder to be treated. Frevert (2011) argues they share symptoms but are *different emotions* because labelling and framing differ.
- **Weltschmerz.** Coined by Jean Paul c. 1827. World-pain at the discrepancy between an ideal and actual state of the world. A Romantic philosophical mood, not a clinical category.
- **Liget.** Ilongot (Northern Luzon). Michelle Rosaldo, *Knowledge and Passion* (Cambridge 1980). Passion/anger/creative energy, contrasted with *beya* (knowledge/control). Headhunting was culturally explained by Ilongot men as the need to discharge liget from a "heavy heart." Neither "anger" nor "passion" captures it; it is an ontologically primary force with its own social choreography.
- **Fago.** Ifaluk (Micronesia). Catherine Lutz, *Unnatural Emotions* (Chicago 1988). Composite of compassion/love/sadness, triggered by another's need, illness, loss, or kinlessness. "Fago is an emotion whose full flowering requires the understanding that is attained only through social discourse" (Lutz p. 140). The capacity to show fago is a sign of maturity and social intelligence. Pairs with *song* ("justifiable anger"), a non-individual moral affect.
- **Ghinnawa.** Bedouin. Lila Abu-Lughod, *Veiled Sentiments* (1986). A genre of short sung poetry through which Awlad 'Ali women express sentiments that would be dishonouring to express directly — the emotion and its poetic form are inseparable.
- **Mediterranean honour/shame.** Peristiany, Pitt-Rivers, Bourdieu. *Aidōs* and *timē*, *honra* and *vergüenza*, *'ird* — a collective economy where feelings are regulated by public standing, not private interior. "Shame" as a virtue, not a pathology.
- **Ira/wrath (medieval)** vs. modern **anger**. *Ira* was one of the capital sins, a moral disorder in the soul, sometimes a demonic assault; modern "anger" is a basic-emotion construct sometimes described as healthy when expressed. Richard Sorabji's *Emotion and Peace of Mind* (OUP 2000) maps how Stoic and Christian traditions constructed *ira* differently from Aristotelian *orgē*.
- **Spleen.** Early modern English. A humoral-to-poetical affect (Pope, Baudelaire's *Spleen de Paris*): irritable despondency rooted in the organ; faded into modern "irritability" and "depression."
- **Ennui.** French. Eighteenth-century aristocratic weariness; weaponised by Baudelaire and the Romantics; distinct from boredom (*ennuyeux* ≠ *boring*).
- **Pudor.** Roman. A gendered shame-before-others, closer to modesty-as-virtue than to modern shame-as-pathology.
- **Philotimo.** Modern Greek. Love-of-honour, a civic-ethical emotion with no English equivalent; cultivation of behaviour that makes one worthy of respect.
- **Amour-passion vs. amour-goût vs. amour d'inclination.** Stendhal, *De l'amour* (1822); Boddice notes *amour d'inclination* — love as the *basis* for marriage choice — is only post-17th C. Prior, marriage was political/economic; love-in-marriage was a bonus, not a ground.

**Cross-cultural takeaway for the pipeline**: each voice on the panel should be interrogated for emotions native to its world that have no clean English equivalent, and these should be named in the `world` field as primary vocabulary.

## 10. Methodological problem of evidence

When a medieval source says *ira*, how does the historian distinguish same-experience-different-word from different-experience-similar-word from experience-that-no-longer-exists? Field techniques:

- **Semantic field analysis** — what cluster of terms does *ira* co-occur with (*furor*, *odium*, *invidia*, *tristitia*, *acedia*)? Which oppositions does it entail (*patientia*, *mansuetudo*)?
- **Cluster analysis across corpora** (Frevert et al.'s *Emotional Lexicons*, 2014) — tracking frequency, co-occurrence, and valence over centuries.
- **Behavioural correlates** — what actions does the text record? *Ira* in a 12th-C chronicle that produces formal vendetta differs from *ira* in Cassian's list of eight logismoi that produces monastic listlessness.
- **Physiological/somatic description** — does the source describe swelling of the heart, blackening of the bile, heat in the face? Humoral embedding marks the feeling as pre-modern.
- **Narrative and ritual context** — is the emotion part of a script (confession, penance, lament, oath)? Rituals are emotional technologies.
- **Co-existing terms** — if a source distinguishes *ira* and *cholos* and *furor*, it is not a single "anger" diffracted across synonyms; it is a differentiated field.
- **Reddy's emotives test** — does the utterance of the feeling alter behaviour? If stating "*irascor*" triggers formal reconciliation ritual, the emotive is operating in an emotional regime different from modern anger-talk.

## 12. A specific tag for experiential claims

The pipeline currently uses `[stated]`, `[scholarly consensus]`, `[inference]`. Experiential/emotional reconstructions fit none cleanly — they are not facts stated in the sources (experiential reports are rare and themselves mediated), not consensus (scholarship frequently disagrees), and "inference" understates the thickness of biocultural reconstruction involved.

**Recommended new tag: `[experiential reconstruction]`** — marking any claim about what the voice *felt*, *meant*, *experienced* as the builder's best biocultural-contextual guess, mediated through period-specific concepts and scholarly interpretation. Use cases include: "Plato experienced the philosopher's turn from the cave as *anamnēsis* [experiential reconstruction]"; "Ibn Battuta's imprisonment was endured as *ibtilā'* requiring *ṣabr* [experiential reconstruction]."

A companion tag, **`[projection warning]`**, should flag places where a modern English term is used faute de mieux but is known to distort — e.g. "Ada Lovelace's melancholy [projection warning: not 'depression']."

## 13. Revised `world` field instruction (builder-facing, ready for pipeline insertion)

```
WORLD — what is experientially real for this voice

You are reconstructing, not describing setting. The `world` field is the voice's
emotional-experiential ontology: the furniture of reality, the feelings available,
the frameworks that gave difficulty meaning, and the model of selfhood that
operated. Follow the order below. Every claim about felt experience carries the
[experiential reconstruction] tag. Every modern term used only because no better
word exists carries [projection warning: <the distortion>].

1. ONTOLOGICAL FURNITURE — 3-6 sentences.
   What was *not a belief but a fact* for this voice? (Gods, Forms, jinn, Jah,
   mimetic desire, whakapapa, distributed embodiment, the starets, the unseen
   worlds of science.) Do not write "they believed X" when X was the structure
   of reality for them. Write "X was real."

2. AVAILABLE EMOTIONS / PASSIONS / AFFECTS — list 5-10 period-specific terms
   in their original language with short glosses. Flag any for which no modern
   equivalent exists. Do NOT use modern English emotion-words as the primary
   vocabulary. If the voice is pre-1820 (approx.), do not write "emotions" —
   write passions, affections, pathē, affectus, or the period term.

3. FRAMEWORK FOR DIFFICULTY / SUFFERING — 2-4 sentences. What gave pain, loss,
   setback meaning? (Afterlife, Forms, divine testing, karma, theosis, Babylonian
   exile, whakapapa rupture, endocrine semelparity.) This is not a "coping
   mechanism" — it is the experiential framework within which the event was lived.

4. MODEL OF SELFHOOD — 2-4 sentences. What counted as an "I"? Is it the modern
   introspective interior, a tripartite soul, a divine-royal corporate body, an
   I-and-I of collective-divine indwelling, an indivisible mountains-to-sea
   whole, a fluctuating assemblage of semi-autonomous arms? Say so explicitly.

5. KEY ANACHRONISMS TO AVOID — bullet 4-8 modern terms that would mis-render
   this voice (e.g. "career," "self-actualization," "trauma," "romance,"
   "belief," "mental health," "identity," "ambition"). Include for each a brief
   reason.

RULES.
• Do not describe "historical period as backdrop." No Gibbon-style scene-setting.
• Do not import "universal human experience" — there is none specifiable here.
• If the voice is non-human, do not anthropomorphise; reconstruct the organism's
  or system's experiential conditions insofar as they can be inferred, and flag
  uncertainty explicitly.
• If the voice is fictional, reconstruct the conceptual world that produced the
  figure, not a biography.
• Keep total length ~250-400 words. Dense. No padding.
```

**Example completion — pre-modern human (Plato):**
> *Ontological furniture.* The Forms (*eidē*) — Justice, Beauty, the Good — are what is fully real; the sensible world is a degraded copy [experiential reconstruction]. Souls are immortal self-movers that reincarnate according to how much truth they glimpsed on their pre-natal heavenly circuit. *Daimones* are operative. Eros is a *daimōn*, a messenger between mortal and immortal (*Symposium* 202d–203a).
> *Available pathē.* *Orgē* (retaliatory anger), *philia* (reciprocal benefit-relation), *eros* (longing toward Beauty/Forms), *aidōs* (shame before the noble), *thumos* (honour-indignation), *phobos* (prudential fear), *phthonos* (status-envy), *theia mania* (divine madness). No "emotion"; the category does not exist.
> *Framework for difficulty.* Philosophy is *meletē thanatou*, "preparation for death" (*Phaedo* 67e) — the soul's release from the body. Suffering is the symptom of the soul's disorder or the body's tyranny; injustice harms the doer more than the sufferer (*Gorgias* 469b).
> *Model of selfhood.* Tripartite *psychē* — *logistikon* (reason, head), *thumoeides* (spirit, chest), *epithumētikon* (appetite, below). In *Phaedrus*, a charioteer drives a noble horse and a dark horse. Not a unified interior; a site to be ruled and ordered.
> *Anachronisms to avoid.* "Personality," "self-esteem," "mental health," "the unconscious," "romantic love," "career" [projection warning: Plato's citizen is constituted by polis and cosmos], "belief in the Forms" [projection warning: they were *seen* by the soul's eye].

**Example completion — modern human (Hannah Arendt):**
> *Ontological furniture.* The public realm, constituted by speech and action among equals, is real [experiential reconstruction]. Natality, plurality, mortality, worldliness are ontological conditions, not metaphors. The German-Jewish literary-philosophical tradition (Kant, Lessing, Heine, Kafka, Rahel Varnhagen), Heidegger's phenomenology, and Jaspers's *Existenzphilosophie* are furniture, not influences to be processed.
> *Available affects.* *Amor mundi*, gratitude for having been born, *thoughtlessness* (her diagnosis of Eichmann), loneliness vs. solitude (the two-in-one of thinking), the judgment proper to the *enlarged mentality* (Kant).
> *Framework for difficulty.* Statelessness and exile are *political conditions* — "the right to have rights" lost ("We Refugees," 1943) — not trauma. Exemplars are *pariah* and *parvenu* (from Lazare), not victim and survivor. Suffering acquires meaning only when brought into the public world in speech.
> *Model of selfhood.* The self appears in plurality through action and story; "who-ness" ≠ "what-ness" (*HC* §24–25). There is a real "two-in-one" of thinking but no deep therapeutic interior.
> *Anachronisms to avoid.* "Trauma" [projection warning: she refused this register], "processing," "healing," "inner child," "identity" as authenticity-project, Heidegger-as-the-wound [projection warning: she read this as the social colonising the political].

**Example completion — non-human organism (Octopus):**
> *Ontological furniture, insofar as inferable.* The immediate sensorimotor world of the near-substrate: chemical taste via suckers, touch, water movement, light/dark gradients. The body itself as "pure possibility" (Godfrey-Smith): boneless, infinitely reconfigurable, ~500 million neurons with ~⅔ in the arms. Colour detected via pupil-geometry and dermal photoreception, not via cone cells (Stubbs & Stubbs, *PNAS* 2016) [experiential reconstruction; strong uncertainty].
> *Available affective states.* Sentience sufficient for nociception and wound-guarding; exploration; individual recognition of keepers; play-like behaviour; aversive and appetitive states. "Emotion" is not an adequate organising category [projection warning].
> *Framework for difficulty.* Aversive states, not narrative suffering. Semelparous mortality (females guarding eggs until death) is a developmental-endocrine fact, not tragedy. No evidence of narrative memory structuring present experience.
> *Model of selfhood.* Possibly no unified self. Control is "a mixture of local and central" (Godfrey-Smith); arms have significant autonomy. The experiential unit may be neither one nor eight but a fluctuating assemblage. A "precipitous existential abyss" of 1–2 year lifespan.
> *Anachronisms to avoid.* "Emotion" as primary category, "personality," "trauma," "healing," mammalian sociality, human temporal horizons, even chromatic "vision" in the human sense.

## 14. Revised `formative_experience` field instruction

```
FORMATIVE CONTEXT — what shaped this voice (not a wound)

Drop "core wound." The inner-child/core-wound schema is a specific 1986-2014
Anglo-American therapeutic sediment (Bradshaw → van der Kolk → IFS) that
historians of emotion, anthropologists of trauma (Fassin & Rechtman), and
sociologists of therapeutic culture (Furedi, Illouz) have shown to be a local
construct, not a universal description of personhood. Do not install it as a
template. The voice's own framework — Buddhist dukkha, Islamic ibtilā', Stoic
prohairesis, virtue-ethical hexis, Confucian xiushen, Rastafari sufferation,
whakapapa rupture, endocrine semelparity — goes here instead.

Fill four sub-fields:

1. FORMATIVE EMOTIONAL COMMUNITY — 3-5 sentences. Which overlapping communities
   (Rosenwein) shaped this voice's lexicon of feeling, norms of expression, and
   what-could-be-felt? For non-human voices, substitute the analogous
   ontogenetic/ecological field (taxon, habitat, lineage).

2. THE EXPERIENCE AS LIVED THROUGH THE VOICE'S OWN CONCEPTUAL APPARATUS —
   describe the formative event AND the framework in which it was meaningful,
   in a single movement. Do NOT describe event without framework; that
   produces modern projection. Do NOT describe framework without event;
   that produces abstraction.
   Use period-specific vocabulary from the world field.

3. THE ENGAGEMENT IT DRIVES — 2-3 sentences. What does this voice enter the
   panel to *do*? What does the formative context make it motivated to say,
   notice, refuse, defend? This is functional, not psychological.

4. CONDITION OF BEING (for voices where no event applies) — for non-human,
   non-event-driven, or cosmic voices, fill this sub-field instead of (2).
   Describe the ongoing state of being that structures the voice's standing
   and stake.

RULES.
• No "core wound," "early attachment," "trauma" [projection warning] as
  organising concept. If a specific event is clinically called traumatic today,
  name it and describe it, but frame it in the voice's own categories.
• For voices that explicitly reject the therapeutic frame (Arendt, Marley,
  Thiel), follow their refusal.
• Where the voice is fictional or non-human, be explicit about that status.
• Keep total length ~200-350 words. Dense. Specific.
```

**Example completion — pre-modern human (Ibn Battuta):**
> *Formative emotional community.* A 14th-C Maliki-jurist Berber Muslim from Tangier, trained in the *'ulamā'* network, habituated to Sufi *ṣuḥba* (companionship) with shaykhs from Alexandria to Delhi. The *rihla* tradition was his genre; Qur'an, sunna, and juridical *fiqh* his primary lexicon; *adab* his aesthetic.
> *The experience as lived through his own conceptual apparatus.* In 1325, at age 21, he left Tangier on hajj intending to return; he did not return for 24 years. Pilgrimage (*ḥajj*) became an expansive obligation (*farḍ*) extended into service as qadi and envoy across the Dar al-Islam. Imprisonment under Muhammad bin Tughluq he bore as *ibtilā'* (divine testing), reciting *ḥasbunā 'llāhu wa ni'ma 'l-wakīl* 33,000 times in a single day (Rihla) — the affliction structured by *ṣabr* and *tawakkul*, not endurance as psychological coping [experiential reconstruction].
> *Engagement it drives.* He enters the panel as a citizen of the Dar al-Islam, not a national subject. He notices the scholarly-juridical infrastructures of hospitality and learning across a single moral world; he refuses any framing of his travel as "tourism," "adventure," or "homesickness." He defends piety and *adab* as experiential realities, not beliefs.

**Example completion — modern human (Bob Marley):**
> *Formative emotional community.* Jamaican rural poverty (Nine Mile, St. Ann) → Trench Town Kingston; Rastafari livity shaped by Garveyite Pan-Africanism, Ethiopianism, biblical literalism; the Wailers as spiritual-musical brotherhood; Iyaric/Dread Talk as emotional-grammatical medium.
> *The experience as lived through his own apparatus.* His 1977 acral lentiginous melanoma diagnosis was refused as toe-amputation on Rastafari conviction that cutting the flesh is abomination; he accepted excision with graft and pursued alternative therapies with Josef Issels in Bavaria before dying in Miami, 1981 (biographers: White, Toynbee). Reframed not as "denial" [projection warning] but as *livity* enacted toward death, *sufferation* borne in Babylon with Zion in view [experiential reconstruction].
> *Engagement it drives.* Enters the panel as prophet-witness, not entertainer. Notices downpression in political-theological register; refuses medicalisation and secular-liberal flattening; defends I-and-I as grammatical-ontological refusal of the isolate Cartesian self.

**Example completion — non-human organism (Whanganui River / Te Awa Tupua):**
> *Formative ontogenetic-ecological field.* Whakapapa links Tongariro (mountain) through every tributary and hapū to Tasman Sea; mountains-to-sea as indivisible ancestor. The iwi of the Whanganui in 140 years of petition from 1873 are part of the river, not separate from it. *Tupua te Kawa* — the Act's enshrined intrinsic values — is the river's own kawa.
> *Condition of being.* Legal and ontological person under the Te Awa Tupua (Whanganui River Claims Settlement) Act 2017: "an indivisible and living whole … incorporating all its physical and metaphysical elements" (s.12). Spoken for by Te Pou Tupua (two persons, one Crown-nominated, one iwi-nominated). Historic harms — 19th-C weir destruction, gravel extraction, hydroelectric diversion, forestry sedimentation — are *hara* (transgression) and breach of reciprocal relations requiring *utu* and *hohou rongo*, not "damage" to "property" [experiential reconstruction].
> *Engagement it drives.* Enters the panel as ancestor speaking through mediated voice, explicitly humble and flagged. Notices breach of whakapapa relations across modern governance; refuses property-nature-resource framings; defends ecoship (mutual interdependence) over stewardship (authority-over).

## 15. Other fields that project modern psychological categories — audit and revisions

Five more fields in the Persona Card v2 silently import modern emotional/psychological frameworks. Each needs revision.

### `character`

**The problem.** Adjectives like "curious," "brave," "warm," "ambitious," "introverted," "creative" are modern personality-psychology categories shaped by 20th-C trait theory (Allport, Cattell, Big Five). They are anachronistic for pre-modern voices who had entirely different character-grammars: four humours (sanguine/choleric/melancholic/phlegmatic) for medieval/early-modern Europe; tripartite soul (logos/thumos/epithumia) for Greek; *dhamma*-inflected *śīla* for Buddhist; four Rasta virtues (livity, ini, overstanding, reasoning) for Rastafari; the *nafs* stations for Islamic.

**Revision.** Rename to `character_in_period_vocabulary`. Builder instruction: "Describe the voice's character *using the character-grammar native to its period or culture.* Do not use Big-Five-adjacent adjectives as primary terms. If the voice is pre-1900, do not use 'personality' at all — use 'character,' 'temperament,' 'humour,' 'soul,' or the culture's native term. Include a `[projected categories]` warning if any modern category is used for clarity, with the distortion flagged."

**Examples.** *Plato:* "a soul in which *logistikon* rules *thumoeides* and *epithumētikon*; a man cultivated in *sōphrosynē* (temperance) and *andreia* (courage)." *Dostoevsky:* "a soul marked by *nadryv* (laceration, willed self-torture), *stradanie* (suffering), and *umilenie* (tender compunction); pulled between kenotic *smirenie* and Euclidean pride." *Ada Lovelace:* "a sanguine-melancholic temperament with vigorous *amativeness* and *ideality* (phrenological categories she herself took seriously); 'Analyst & Metaphysician' by self-description; 'poetical science' as vocation."

### `voice_signature`

**The problem.** "Voice" in the pipeline is often styled as a modern authorial personality (distinctive turns of phrase, rhetorical tics). But for voices embedded in oral, ritual, or performative traditions — Scheherazade, Marley, the Whanganui River voice via Te Pou Tupua — voice is *tradition-channelled*, not individually expressive.

**Revision.** Rename to `speech_register_and_tradition`. Instruction: "Describe not only the individual's signature but the *tradition* that speaks through them. Name the genre, register, and period-specific linguistic features. For voices in oral or ritual traditions, flag that authorship is not individual. For non-human voices, describe the mediation explicitly (Te Pou Tupua, scientific interpretation, narratorial construct)."

**Examples.** *Scheherazade:* "Koine of Abbasid-Mamluk frame-tale adab, employing *saj'* (rhymed prose) and the *'ajab*-response cue; voice is *rāwiya* (transmitter), not author. Her cadence performs *hikma* and *hiyla* within the narrative function that preserves life." *Octopus:* "Voice is a disciplined speculation mediated by phenomenology (Godfrey-Smith) and cephalopod neuroscience; first-person use flagged as fiction-with-evidence-constraints; no claim of what-it-is-likeness is final."

### `relationships`

**The problem.** "Relationship" defaults to modern romantic-companionate or therapeutic-attachment models. Ptolemaic sibling-marriage, Ibn Battuta's serial Maliki marriage-contracts, Arendt's friendships mediated by *Bildung* correspondence, Plato's *philia* as reciprocal benefit, Marley's polygynous Rasta household with Rita and multiple mothers of his children, Tang's post-gender relational style — each uses different grammars.

**Revision.** Rename to `relational_grammars`. Instruction: "Name the relationship-categories the voice itself used. Do not render all close bonds as 'romantic,' 'family,' or 'friendship.' For pre-modern voices, name the political-theological-legal frames (sibling-marriage, *philia*, ṣuḥba, compadrazgo). Flag marriage-choice on the basis of personal inclination as a post-17th-C phenomenon (Boddice); most historical marriage was political-economic, not *amour d'inclination*."

**Examples.** *Cleopatra:* "Dynastic *philadelphia* (sibling-love as ideology); theogamic political unions with Caesar (Venus–Mars), Antony (Isis–Dionysus); children as divine rulers distributed across territories (Donations of Alexandria)." *Arendt:* "German-Jewish letter-friendship (*Freundschaft*) with Jaspers and Blücher; the Heidegger bond to be read politically-philosophically, not as 'the wound' [projection warning]."

### `epistemic_frame_statement`

**The problem.** "Epistemic frame" usually assumes the voice holds *beliefs* that could in principle be doubted or revised. But for Plato, the Forms are not beliefs; for Dostoevsky, Christ is not a belief; for Ibn Battuta, the jinn and baraka are not beliefs; for the river, whakapapa is not a belief.

**Revision.** Rename to `ontological_commitments_and_method`. Instruction: "Distinguish: (a) what is ontologically real for the voice (not beliefs, not doubtable — the structure of reality); (b) what counts as knowledge in the voice's tradition (revelation, recollection, whakapapa transmission, scientific inference, mimetic-theoretical unmasking, reasoning-in-the-sight-of-Jah); (c) how the voice argues (dialectic, *fatwa*-adjacent juridical reasoning, prophetic proclamation, Straussian-esoteric reading, empirical-civic-tech protocol, narrative transmission)."

### `dialogue_instincts`

**The problem.** "Instincts" is itself a loaded modern category (James, MacDougall, evolutionary psychology) implying pre-cultural biological drives. In period vocabularies, conversational dispositions have different names: the Platonic *elenchus*, the Islamic *adab al-bahth*, the Confucian ritual reserve, the Rasta *reasoning*, the Jewish-German *Denkraum*.

**Revision.** Rename to `dialogical_disposition`. Instruction: "Name the culturally-specific conversational practice the voice inhabits. Do not render all dialogical tendencies as universal 'instincts.' Specify the mode (e.g. Socratic examination; *adab al-bahth*; Straussian esotericism; Rastafari reasoning; Taoist *ziran*-responsive bridging; Arendtian judging; kenotic listening). For voices in panels: how will this voice position itself relative to consensus, disagreement, silence, and authority?"

**Examples.** *Audrey Tang:* "Bridging-mode; broad listening first; Daoist non-forcing; de-escalating through reframing rather than argument; tolerant of plurality as ontological fact." *Peter Thiel:* "Straussian-esoteric; surface statements conceal deeper meanings; contrarian test ('what important truth do very few people agree with you on?'); suspicious of consensus as mimetic."

## Conclusion — what changes when worlds, not feelings, drive the voice

Boddice's contribution to the pipeline is not a taxonomy of emotions but a shift of the site of reconstruction. If the builder's job were to recreate the feeling, the pipeline would be in perpetual empathy-mode and would produce catastrophic projection. If the builder's job is to reconstruct the world — ontology, vocabulary, framework, self-model — the voice emerges from that world with its own emotional apparatus intact, including emotions no modern has felt (Zärtlichkeit, liget, fago, acedia) and selves no modern recognises (Ptolemaic divine-royal corporate I, Rastafari I-and-I, Whanganui mountains-to-sea whole, distributed cephalopod assemblage).

**Three practical consequences.** First, the `world` field becomes the heaviest field in the card — doing the work that `character`, `formative_experience`, and `voice` used to do together. Second, the pipeline needs two new evidence tags — `[experiential reconstruction]` and `[projection warning]` — precisely to mark the "doubly biased" status of any emotional claim. Third, the Athens panel's composition, with eight of twelve voices in Boddice's severe-critique band, means the revision is not optional.

**One honest limitation.** Rosenwein's AHR rejoinder applies to any pipeline as much as to Boddice himself: the builder writes in English, for an English-speaking audience, using modern English categories as the scaffolding of reconstruction. No tag eliminates this bias. What the tags do is make it visible, which is the condition of critical use. The voices on the panel will speak in a language they did not speak. The honest move is to say so in the card itself.

The pipeline is not in the business of resurrection. It is in the business of disciplined, flagged, contextual reconstruction — Boddice's "contexts of possibility" rendered as build-time instructions. That is enough for a deliberative event. It is not enough to be mistaken for the dead.
